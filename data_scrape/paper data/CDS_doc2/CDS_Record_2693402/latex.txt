# Trigger menu in 2018

The ATLAS Collaboration

###### Abstract

This document summarises the trigger menu deployed by the ATLAS experiment during 2018 data-taking of both proton-proton and lead-lead collisions. Modifications and improvements relative to the proton-proton 2017 trigger menu are discussed, for nominal running conditions of \(2.0\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\) and an average of 58 interactions per bunch crossing. Additionally, trigger menus for special proton-proton running configurations are described. The trigger menu for lead-lead collisions is discussed in detail.

## 1 Introduction

The trigger system is an essential component of any hadron collider experiment as it is responsible for deciding whether or not to record a given beam crossing for later study. During the second experimental running period (Run 2) of the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), the ATLAS trigger system [1] operated efficiently at proton-proton (\(pp\)) collision centre-of-mass energies of \(\sqrt{s}=13\) TeV and \(\sqrt{s}=5.02\) TeV. The LHC operated with instantaneous luminosities of up to \(0.5\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\) in 2015, up to \(1.4\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\) in 2016, and up to \(1.7\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\) in 2017, during 13 TeV \(pp\) collisions. A detailed description of the initial performance of the trigger system during 2015 is summarised in Ref. [2]. Summaries of the 2016 and 2017 trigger menus and performance are provided in Ref. [3] and Ref. [4], respectively.

This document summarises the trigger menus used during 2018. Both \(pp\) data-taking up to a peak luminosity of over \(2.0\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\) and lead-lead (Pb+Pb) data-taking up to a peak luminosityof \(6.2\times 10^{27}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\) are addressed. Improvements of the trigger system and algorithms with respect to the previously described performance in 2017 [4] are described for \(pp\) data-taking.

## 2 The ATLAS detector and trigger system

The ATLAS detector is a multipurpose particle physics detector with nearly \(4\pi\) coverage in solid angle around the collision point,1 and is described in detail in Ref. [5].

Footnote 1: ATLAS uses a right-handed coordinate system with its origin at the nominal interaction point (IP) in the centre of the detector and the \(z\)-axis along the beam pipe. The \(x\)-axis points from the IP to the centre of the LHC ring, and the \(y\)-axis points upwards. Cylindrical coordinates \((r,\phi)\) are used in the transverse plane, \(\phi\) being the azimuthal angle around the \(z\)-axis. The pseudorapidity is defined in terms of the polar angle \(\theta\) as \(\eta=-\ln\tan(\theta/2)\). Transverse momentum and energy are defined as \(p_{\rm T}=p\sin\theta\) and \(E_{\rm T}=E\sin\theta\), respectively. Angular distance is measured in units of \(\Delta R\equiv\sqrt{(\Delta\eta)^{2}+(\Delta\phi)^{2}}\).

Since the start of Run 2, the ATLAS detector has had a two-level trigger system. The first-level trigger (Level-1 trigger, L1) is implemented in hardware and uses a subset of the detector information to reduce the rate of accepted events from an input rate of up to 40 MHz to maximally 100 kHz. The L1 topological trigger processor (L1Topo) [6] combines information about several objects into topological information about the event, and is used to greatly enhance the background rejection at L1 by exploiting the topological features of the targeted signal process.

This is followed by a software-based trigger (high-level trigger, HLT) that reduces the rate of recorded events. An average rate of \(\sim 1.2\) kHz is written out for physics analysis. HLT objects, such as leptons or jets, are only reconstructed when specified L1 triggers have accepted an event. This event reconstruction is referred to as online reconstruction. Events that are accepted by the HLT are written out into different streams and transferred to CERN's Tier-0 computing centre for offline reconstruction.

Event selections in the HLT are referred to as triggers, and the collection of all L1 and HLT triggers and their prescales is called the trigger menu. Triggers are comprised of steps, where a step is a sequence of algorithms. A typical step will execute multiple feature-extraction algorithms requesting event-data fragments from within a rectangular (in \(\eta\times\phi\)) Region of Interest (RoI) defined by the L1 output, and run reconstruction algorithms over this sub-set of the event data. Each step terminates on a hypothesis algorithm, which uses the reconstructed features to form a boolean decision whether the trigger condition is satisfied. Triggers can also comprise a single hypothesis algorithm that makes no additional selection on an event after the L1 trigger; triggers with this selection are referred to as pass-through. The output of single algorithms and entire steps is cached, as is the partial-event data requested by feature extraction algorithms, and any subsequent requests for the same algorithm/step (within the same RoI) or data fragment(s) within the event processing returns the local cached version. Each L1 and HLT trigger has an associated prescale \(p_{\rm L1}\) and \(p_{\rm HLT}\), respectively, which take values \(\geq 1\). Only \(1/p_{\rm L1}\) events passing by a L1 trigger accepted for HLT processing and only \(1/p_{\rm HLT}\) events passing the L1 selection required by an HLT trigger are processed at the HLT. Triggers with both \(p_{\rm L1}=1\) and \(p_{\rm HLT}=1\) are referred to as unprescaled.

## 3 Trigger menu for 2018 \(pp\) collisions

During the 2018 \(pp\) run, the LHC delivered a peak luminosity close to \(L=2.0\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\), and briefly reached \(L=2.1\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\). The runs with peak luminosity were achieved with 2544 colliding bunches, and had a peak number of average interactions per bunch crossing (pileup) of 56 at \(L=2.0\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\) (59 at \(L=2.1\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\)), where the average is performed over all colliding bunch pairs. Given the expected luminosity was not significantly higher with respect to the 2017 running conditions, only minimal changes to the trigger menu were required and implemented. This consistency greatly eases the use of the full Run-2 dataset for physics analyses.

Even though the instantaneous luminosity was consistently higher in 2018 than in 2017, when the luminosity was levelled at \(1.56\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\), the peak pileup was consistently lower. This was due to the LHC having resolved the issues that enforced a maximum of 1866 colliding bunches, which was compensated for by increasing the number of protons per bunch, and thus the pileup [4]. The rates and/or CPU consumption of some triggers are exceptionally sensitive to the pileup conditions. Thus, at equivalent luminosities, the lower pileup in 2018 reduced the CPU usage and overall output rate, freeing resources for additional developments.

The trigger menu strategy is based on the following building blocks:

* Primary triggers, which are used for physics measurements, and typically run unprescaled.
* Support triggers, which are used for efficiency and performance measurements, background estimates or monitoring, and typically run at a small rate (of the order of 0.5 Hz each, with prescales). About 15% of the HLT bandwidth is dedicated to support triggers.
* Alternative triggers, which run alternative online reconstruction algorithms complementary to primary and/or support triggers, and are used, for example, as part of the commissioning of future primary triggers.
* Backup triggers, with tighter selections and lower processing or output rate, in case the CPU usage or output rate of the primary trigger becomes too high.
* Calibration triggers, which are used for detector calibrations, and often run at high rates that are compensated by storing only the necessary detector information for each event, thus reducing the event size and overall bandwidth.

The primary triggers that comprise the 2018 standard physics trigger menu, as optimised for the \(1.7-2.0\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\) peak instantaneous luminosity regime, and their measured rates are shown in Table 1. The different particle identification (PID) and isolation definitions per object can be found in Ref. [2].

Recorded events are gathered in data streams, depending on their primary use case and their specific offline reconstruction needs. The main physics stream contains data from the full detector when a trigger is activated. However, some other streams, such as those used for detector calibration, only record partial information, a strategy called partial event building (PEB). In PEB, only specific parts of the detector or subdetectors are stored, which can reduce the event size by a large factor. An alternative approach to reduce the event size is followed by the trigger-level analysis (TLA) [7; 8], where only the objects reconstructed by the HLT are stored. By reducing the size of the event data, the triggers can operate at much higher rates, without saturation of the output bandwidth. However, other limitations, such as CPU resources and readout rates, prohibit arbitrarily high rates even with minimal partial event information. The smallest event size recorded in 2018 was for AFP calibration data, at 1.3 kB per event, compared to an average full event size of 1 MB per event. ### Trigger menus for special \(pp\) runs

Additional trigger menus were designed to be used during special runs. These menus are adapted to the particular data-taking conditions and physics purpose of each run.

**Start-up menu:**: During the first weeks of 2018 data-taking (17 April-6 May), the LHC initially operated with many fewer bunches than nominal and thus at much lower instantaneous luminosities. The luminosity progressively increased up to the nominal run conditions. This period is referred to as start-up, and a dedicated menu was deployed, with the main focus being the commissioning and validation of new triggers, algorithms, and improvements introduced during the end-of-year shutdown. Dedicated triggers were also included for detector, calibration and performance studies.
**van der Meer scan menu:**: The absolute luminosity calibration of the ATLAS detector is performed via a van der Meer (vdM) scan [9]. During the vdM scan runs, events were recorded based on activity in the MBTS (Minimum Bias Trigger Scintillators) and LUCID (LUminosity measurement using a Cherenkov Integrating Detector) subsystems. The events were recorded to a dedicated stream with PEB at a rate of approximately 20 kHz.
**\(\beta^{*}\) = 90 m menu:**: Six days of \(pp\) data-taking with \(\beta^{*}\) = 90 m took place from 2 - 7 July. The programme was divided in two parts: the first part was for elastic physics with 100 ns bunch spacing for cross-section measurements, while the second part was for diffractive physics with 50 ns bunch spacing mainly for glueball and odderon searches. Triggers dedicated to elastic and diffractive collisions were deployed in both scenarios. The elastic triggers are based on protons detected with ALFA [10] at L1 and are directly recorded without further HLT selection. The diffractive triggers select minimum-bias (MB) events, soft dimuon, diphoton, and dijet events at the HLT, on top of L1 ALFA selections.
**900 GeV ALFA menu:**: During 11 - 14 October, several runs were recorded for the elastic measurement programme of ATLAS with a centre-of-mass energy of 900 GeV. ## 4 Improvements and updates to the trigger during 2018

Before and during 2018 data-taking, changes were made to both L1 and HLT triggers. These include improvements to lower output rates without impacting efficiencies and the addition of L1 and HLT triggers to select new topologies.

### Improvements to the L1 trigger

#### 4.1.1 L1 muon trigger

The L1 muon trigger requires spatial and temporal coincidence of hits in resistive plate chambers (RPCs) or thin gap chambers (TGCs) for muons in the barrel (\(|\eta|<1.05\)) and endcap (\(1.05<|\eta|<2.4\)), respectively. The muon \(p_{\rm T}\) is estimated by using the degree of deviation from the hit pattern of an infinite momentum assumption. In Run 1, the L1 muon trigger rates in the endcap regions were dominated by low-\(p_{\rm T}\) charged particles originating from low-\(p_{\rm T}\) backgrounds.

To suppress such fake events, an additional coincidence of the TGCs was implemented on 21 May 2018. Muon candidates in the region \(1.05<|\eta|<1.3\) are outside the coverage of the small TGC wheel, and instead pass through the extended barrel of the tile calorimeter (TileCal). Thus, muons in this region are required to have big-wheel TGC hits coincident with energy deposits of minimum-ionising particles in the outermost layer of the tile calorimeter (\(1.05<|\eta|<1.3\)). The additional coincidence reduces the trigger rate by about 6%, as shown in Figure 3, with signal efficiency losses of 2.5% for muons within \(1.05<|\eta|<1.3\).

Additionally, the trigger coincidence window (CW) for the endcap region, \(1.05<|\eta|<2.4\), was optimised for both the 4 GeV (MU4) and 6 GeV (MU6) L1 muon trigger thresholds. This

### Improvements to high-level triggers

#### 4.2.1 Muon

In 2017, the primary single muon trigger demonstrated a decreasing efficiency at high pileup due to the isolation applied to the HLT muons. In order to determine whether or not a muon is isolated, the scalar \(p_{\rm T}\) sum of inner detector (ID) tracks below a specified \(\Delta R\)(track, muon) is calculated. However, since this isolation cone is susceptible to including ID tracks from pileup vertices, an additional requirement is placed on tracks considered for isolation. The difference in longitudinal impact parameters between the track and the muon, \(|\Delta z|\), is restricted below a specified value. In 2018, \(|\Delta z|\) was lowered from 6 mm to 2 mm, to mitigate a decrease in efficiency seen at high pileup. Since decreasing \(|\Delta z|\) means that fewer tracks are used for isolation, the trigger rate did increase. However, a small rate increase was considered acceptable given the corresponding increase in efficiency. The rates and efficiencies for three \(|\Delta z|\) requirements are summarised in Table 2. Efficiencies are computed with respect to isolated offline muons that pass a non-isolated HLT trigger with \(p_{\rm T}>26\) GeV, in simulated \(Z\rightarrow\mu\mu\) events.

#### 4.2.2 Tau

Several improvements were made to tau triggers in 2018, all related to HLT algorithms.

First, the selection of ID tracks pointing to the L1 tau RoI was made more robust at high pileup. The tau trigger _HLT tau25 medium BDT (1-3 fast tracks)_ included in the trigger menu in 2017 required the number of tracks found by the Fast Track Finder (FTF) algorithm to be between 1 and 3 [14]. This led to trigger inefficiencies for 3-prong taus at high pileup, due to the increased probability for the FTF algorithm to reconstruct fake tracks at high ID occupancy, in addition to the 3 tau tracks. The problem was mitigated by considering FTF tracks refitted with precision tracking, which reduces the fake track contribution. This tighter track selection was used for the whole 2018 data-taking period - referred to as _HLT tau25 medium BDT (1-3 precision tracks)_ trigger in Figure 5. The following two additional HLT algorithm improvements were deployed in July 2018.

An energy calibration based on a Boosted Regression Tree has superseded the simpler calibration that corrects for the calorimeter response and the energy from pileup. This multivariate energy calibration is largely based on that used in the offline tau reconstruction, which slightly improves the trigger efficiency for low-\(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) taus. The track association was further tightened to reduce the impact from pileup tracks by reducing the maximum allowed \(|\Delta(z)|\) between the leading and sub-leading tracks from 1 mm to 0.75 mm.

An improved tau PID algorithm based on a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) has superseded the identification algorithm based on a Boosted Decision Tree (BDT). Thanks to the superior jet rejection provided by the RNN algorithm, RNN triggers could be made more efficient for taus than BDT triggers, for equivalent output rates. Dedicated RNN tunes were used for HLT tau candidates with 0, 1, or 2-3 tracks. The RNN trigger is referred to as _HLT tau25 medium RNN (0-3 precision tracks)_ in Figure 5.

The HLT \(E_{\mathrm{T}}^{\mathrm{miss}}\) of the _puffu_ algorithm is calculated as the negative of the transverse momentum vector sum of all calorimeter topological clusters corrected for pileup. The pileup correction is done by grouping the clusters into coarser "towers" which are then marked as pileup if their \(E_{\mathrm{T}}\) falls below a pileup-dependent threshold. A fit to below-threshold towers is performed, taking into account resolutions, making the assumption that the contribution of the pileup to \(E_{\mathrm{T}}^{\mathrm{miss}}\) is zero. The fitted pileup \(E_{\mathrm{T}}\) density is used to correct the above-threshold towers. The second HLT selection in the trigger is on the _cell_ algorithm. The \(E_{\mathrm{T}}^{\mathrm{miss}}\) of this algorithm is calculated as the negative of the transverse momentum vector sum of all calorimeter cells passing a two-sided noise cut.

Detailed studies showed that a large percentage of the online rate is generated by events with comparatively low offline transverse missing energy. These events are mostly not used by physics analyses and rejected by the additional requirement of passing a low _cell_ threshold. The combination of the two algorithms exploits the different correlations between online and offline \(E_{\mathrm{T}}^{\mathrm{miss}}\) of each algorithm. This reduces the \(E_{\mathrm{T}}^{\mathrm{miss}}\) trigger rate coming from noise and energy mismeasurements while preserving events with genuine \(E_{\mathrm{T}}^{\mathrm{miss}}\). The impact of the additional _cell_ selection criteria in the plateau region of the trigger efficiency curve is negligible and very small (<1%) in the turn-on region but it reduces the trigger rate by more than 40%.

#### 4.2.4 \(b\)-jets

The baseline configuration of \(b\)-jet triggers in 2018 used the same flavour-tagging multivariate algorithm tuning that was deployed during the 2017 data-taking period. This was possible due to the general similarity of running conditions between these two years. However, the \(b\)-jet trigger menu also introduced several triggers that used a new tuning of the multivariate algorithm intended to increase \(b\)-jet flavour tagging efficiency for high-\(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) jets (e.g. \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>250\) GeV). This was done using the same tuning procedure adopted in the offline flavour tagging approach and increased the harmonisation between procedures used in the trigger and those used offline.

The baseline training procedure uses a simulation of \(pp\to t\bar{t}\) to develop a BDT (MV2c10) that can provide a measure of the probability that an individual jet arises from the hadronisation of a bottom or charm quark, or light quarks and gluons (henceforth referred to as "light" jets). This simulation provides an ample number of jets of various origins to train such an algorithm, but the \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) range is limited due to the kinematics of the underlying physics process. To complement this training sample's kinematics, an additional simulation sample is introduced into the training procedure, consisting of the process \(pp\to Z^{\prime}\to t\bar{t}\), where the heavy vector boson (\(Z^{\prime}\)) has a mass of 1 TeV and is produced by the generator with a flat \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) spectrum. These samples are interleaved during training to provide consistent exposure of the BDT to high- and low-\(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) jets, and this is referred to as "hybrid tuning". The \(b\)-jet triggers with the new "hybrid tuning" were deployed in August 2018.

#### 4.2.5 \(B\)-physics

Historically, the \(B\)-physics trigger programme in ATLAS has focused on decays with muons. In 2018, a new set of triggers was added to select low-\(p_{\rm T}\) electron events within an invariant mass region corresponding to decays of \(b\)-hadrons. The development focused on \(B^{0}\to K^{*}e^{+}e^{-}\) decays, but all triggers developed are agnostic to additional tracks, and thus are also sensitive to _e.g._\(B^{+}\to K^{+}e^{+}e^{-}\) decays. Both L1 items and HLT algorithms were developed.

Mimicking the \(B\)-physics L1 muon strategy, L1 topological triggers using EM RoIs were developed. The main trigger requires the invariant mass between two L1 EM RoIs, one with \(E_{\rm T}>7\) GeV (EM7) and the second with \(E_{\rm T}>5\) GeV (EM5), to be less than 9 GeV. However, since the granularity of the L1 calorimeter trigger is somewhat coarser than the extent of a single, low-\(p_{\rm T}\) electron, when the two electrons are very close-by they will only create a single L1 EM RoI in the detector. Thus, a L1 trigger for close-by electrons was created, which requires a L1 EM RoI within \(\Delta R<0.3\) of a L1 jet RoI. These two situations are depicted in Figure 9.

Because the production of one \(b\)-hadron is likely to result in the production of another, the muon requirement provides a higher purity sample than simply prescaling the original L1 triggers. #### 4.2.6 Dedicated triggers

A selection of triggers targeting specific, signal-driven topologies were added in 2018 to increase the sensitivity of analyses with hard-to-access phase space not recorded by existing triggers.

**Photon + multijets:**: In gauge-mediated Supersymmetry models a possible final state consists of a photon, several jets, and moderate \(E_{\mathrm{T}}^{\mathrm{miss}}\). The trigger used for this final state until 2018 was a multijet trigger requiring seven jets with a \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>45\GeV\) at HLT. By adding the photon to the final state as a trigger requirement, the number of low \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) jets can be reduced and the jet \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) threshold kept low at the same time. Two triggers were deployed in 2018 for this purpose combining a photon with a \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) threshold of 45 with a requirement of five and six jets with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) above 45 for primary and support, respectively. The expected gain in signal statistics compared to a pure multijet trigger is expected to be around 30%.
**Stopped particle:**: Several theories of physics beyond the Standard Model (BSM) predict heavy long-lived particles. These particles could be slow-moving and decay in the detector after hours or months. To trigger on these out-of-time decays the ATLAS trigger exploits bunch crossings without \(pp\) collisions. A few of these types of triggers have been running online during Run 2. In 2018 an additional trigger was deployed online to improve the number of triggered signal events of the existing triggers. Both the newly introduced and the existing trigger require a central jet with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) greater than 55 and \(E_{\mathrm{T}}^{\mathrm{miss}}>50\GeV\) but the newly introduced one triggers on the five bunch crossings without collisions before and after any bunch crossing with collisions. These bunch crossings are excluded by the existing trigger. Depending on the bunch filling scheme of the LHC the number of additionally

**Low-mass diphoton:**: Several BSM theories predict resonances below the SM Higgs boson mass, for which the diphoton decay mode is the primary mode for discovery. Lowering the photon \(E_{\mathrm{T}}\) thresholds was required to trigger efficiently on the desired phase-space, \(10<m_{\gamma\gamma}<60\) GeV. However, the standard diphoton trigger with two 20 GeV photons was already the lowest possible threshold, primarily restricted by the large L1 rate of low-\(E_{\mathrm{T}}\) L1 EM RoIs. As a compromise, a L1 item with two L1 EM RoIs and a L1 jet RoI was used, with the L1 jet RoI coming from initial-state radiation. With the addition of a jet, the HLT photon \(E_{\mathrm{T}}\) threshold was lowered to 6 GeV. The exact L1 jet threshold was dependent on luminosity: 100 GeV above \(1.2\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\), 50 GeV with prescale of 20 between \(1.0\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\) and \(1.2\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\), and 40 GeV below \(1.0\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\).

At HLT, two photons passing an \(E_{\mathrm{T}}\) threshold of 6 GeV are required. The reconstruction of these photons is seeded from L1 EM RoIs with a \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) threshold of 3 GeV, so if these are not present, HLT photon reconstruction does not proceed. No jet is required in the HLT. Tight PID and isolation are applied to the photons. Additionally, the corresponding triggers with loose photon PID, for performance studies, were added with a constant rate of 0.2 Hz.
**Displaced muon:**: A new single-muon trigger was developed to increase sensitivity to exotic, long-lived neutral particle signatures involving muons produced at a significant distance from the IP. These muons will not produce an ID track, so triggers for these types of signatures use muons with only a muon spectrometer track (MS-only muons). However, the rate of such triggers are incredibly high, especially in the endcaps, and consequently the suitable trigger running in 2015-2017 was restricted to the barrel (\(|\eta|<1.05\), for MS-only muons with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>60\) GeV). The pseudorapidity restriction results in a much lower global signal acceptance for some models, so in 2018 a new trigger was developed to enable acceptance of endcap MS-only muons. Muons with \(1.05<|\eta|<2.4\) are required to have at least three good precision layers, an increase from standard muon triggers, which require only one. To ensure the HLT rate was acceptable, the \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) threshold for muons (at all pseudorapidities) is 80 GeV for this trigger, resulting in a rate of 15 Hz at \(2\times 10^{34}\) cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\). This increased the efficiency for a \(Z^{\prime}\rightarrow\mu\mu\) benchmark signal model with \(m_{Z^{\prime}}=250\) GeV and \(c\tau_{Z^{\prime}}=100\) mm from 37% to 54%.
**Vector boson fusion:**: Triggers specifically targeting vector boson fusion (VBF) final states were introduced in July 2018 and collected an integrated luminosity of 41 fb\({}^{-1}\). They are all seeded from the new L1Topo trigger MJJ-500-NFF, which requires the invariant mass between two jets to be greater than 500 GeV. Since the invariant mass algorithm requiresa large amount of resources from the L1Topo FPGAs, the invariant mass calculation is only performed on a subset of pairs of L1 jet RoIs. The pairs are formed by selecting one object from each of two lists. The first list is the 6 highest \(E_{\mathrm{T}}\) L1 jet RoIs with \(|\eta|<3.2\) and \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>30\) GeV, and the second list contains the 6 highest \(E_{\mathrm{T}}\) L1 jet RoIs with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>20\) GeV, in the full \(\eta\) range (\(|\eta|<4.9\)). The topological requirements on the two jets are sufficient to lower the rate enough to unprescale this item at all luminosities. This L1 topological trigger allows the new VBF triggers to have both lower L1 jet \(E_{\mathrm{T}}\) thresholds and no L1 requirements on any additional objects in the event. At HLT, an inclusive trigger broadly targeting all possible VBF final states only places requirements on HLT jets. It requires one \(E_{\mathrm{T}}>70\) GeV jet with \(|\eta|<3.2\) and a second, \(E_{\mathrm{T}}>50\) GeV jet with \(|\eta|<4.9\). Additionally, at least one pair of jets, with each jet satisfying \(E_{\mathrm{T}}>70\) GeV and \(|\eta|<4.9\), must have an invariant mass \(>1100\) GeV, \(\Delta\phi_{\mathrm{jj}}<2.0\), and \(\Delta\eta_{\mathrm{jj}}>4.0\). The angular requirements \(\Delta\phi_{\mathrm{jj}}<2.0\) and \(\Delta\eta_{\mathrm{jj}}>4.0\) are also applied offline, as in the trigger requirement.

## 5 Trigger menu for 2018 heavy-ion collisions

Heavy-ion (HI) collisions differ significantly from \(pp\) collisions. The intrinsic geometry of nuclear collisions results in large event-by-event variations of both the track multiplicity and the energy density. The centrality of Pb+Pb events is defined using the total transverse energy measured in the forward calorimeter, \(\Sigma E_{\rm T}^{\rm FCal}\)[16]. The \(\Sigma E_{\rm T}^{\rm FCal}\) distribution is divided into percentiles of the total inelastic cross-section for Pb+Pb collisions, with the 0-10% centrality interval classifying the most central collisions (the highest \(\Sigma E_{\rm T}^{\rm FCal}\), the smallest impact parameter which is the distance between the centres of the two colliding nuclei) and the 90-100% classifying the most peripheral collisions (the smallest \(\Sigma E_{\rm T}^{\rm FCal}\), the largest impact parameter). The increase in collision centrality results in an increasing number of binary nucleon-nucleon collisions leading to an increase of per-event yields of hard processes. A large amount of additional energy and low momentum particles coming from the underlying event (UE) is produced in more central collisions. Furthermore, the initial spatial asymmetry of the overlap region in the collision of two nuclei are translated into momentum anisotropies of the final-state particle production, which are experimentally characterised by Fourier (flow) harmonics \(v_{n}\) of the azimuthal angle distributions of produced particles [17; 18].

In addition to the collisions with inelastic hadronic interactions, heavy nuclei also interact electromagnetically when the nuclei have significantly larger impact parameters with no hadronic interaction. The cross-section of the electromagnetic interactions, which include photon-photon and photon-nucleus scattering, is enhanced by the large electric charge of Pb nuclei. These collisions, commonly referred to as ultra-peripheral collisions (UPCs) have typically very small track multiplicity and UE. The effective cross-section of electromagnetic processes in Pb+Pb collisions is approximately 200 b for single Pb electromagnetic dissociation [19]. The cross-section for hadronic interactions is 7.7 b [20].

The nucleons that do not interact in the collision of two heavy ions are known as spectators. The Zero Degree Calorimeter (ZDC) [5], located symmetrically at \(z=\pm 140\) m and covering \(|\eta|>8.3\), is designed to detect spectator neutrons, which follow a straight trajectory after such collisions. HI triggers use signals from both the main detector and spectator neutrons detected in the ZDC. Furthermore, many triggers are modified compared to their \(pp\) counterparts to be able to deal with large event-by-event variations in particle multiplicity.

During the 2018 HI run, the LHC provided Pb+Pb collisions with centre-of-mass energies of \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=5.02\) TeV. The instantaneous luminosity reached up to \(6.2\times 10^{27}\)cm\({}^{-2}\)s\({}^{-1}\), leading to a collision rate of 48 kHz for hadronic interactions and approximately 1.5 MHz for events with single Pb electromagnetic dissociation. The rate of accepted events by L1 entering the HLT was restricted by CPU consumption of some triggers and by readout capabilities of several sub-detectors.

The HI trigger menu has the following main components:

* Triggers selecting hard processes including high \(E_{\rm T}\) jets, \(b\)-jets, muons, electrons, and photons in inelastic Pb+Pb collisions. The recording rate of these hard probe triggersdecreased with the instantaneous luminosity in a fill. These triggers were recorded in a dedicated hard probes stream.
* Minimum-bias (MB) triggers recording full event information, which are split by centrality to streams for peripheral collisions (PCs) and central collisions (CCs). The recording rate of MB triggers increased as a function of time in a fill.
* MB triggers with PEB, which are split by centrality to streams for PCs and CCs, where the calorimetric information (except FCal) is not recorded. The recording rate of MB triggers increased as a function of time in a fill.
* Triggers collecting events with particular global properties. This includes event-shape triggers collecting events with a large initial spatial asymmetry of the collision and ultra central collision (UCC) triggers selecting the most central HI collisions. The recording rate of these triggers was decreased with the instantaneous luminosity in a fill. Event-shape triggers and UCC triggers were recorded into two independent data streams.
* Triggers selecting various signatures in UPCs. The recording rate of the primary UPC triggers decreased with the instantaneous luminosity in a fill. This decrease in the rate was compensated by a corresponding increase in the recording rate of supporting UPC triggers. All UPC events were recorded to a dedicated UPC stream.

### Minimum-bias and global event properties triggers

Triggers selecting inelastic hadronic events with the smallest bias are called MB triggers. The primary trigger selection of MB HI collisions is based on a logical OR between the following three triggers: 1) a very low activity trigger requiring the ZDC coincidence trigger with a veto on total transverse energy in the calorimeter (TE) to be smaller than 50 GeV at L1 and with a requirement of at least one track reconstructed at HLT; 2) a low activity trigger requiring the TE to be above 50 GeV and smaller than 600 GeV at L1; and 3) a CC trigger requiring at least 600 GeV of total transverse energy at L1. The high-purity central and low activity triggers do not require any additional selection at HLT. The ZDC coincidence trigger is not included in the low activity and central collision triggers, as it is not fully efficient in the most central Pb+Pb collisions where all neutrons from one of the nuclei have interacted leaving none free to be measured by the ZDC.

Two independent MB streams were recorded during the 2018 HI run. The first MB stream collected data from all the sub-detectors. The second MB stream used PEB, and collected data from only the ID, muon spectrometer, ZDC, and FCal. The data from the central calorimeter system were dropped, decreasing the overall event size from \(\sim\)2200 kB to \(\sim\)1100 kB for central events and from \(\sim\)1000 kB to \(\sim\)200 kB for peripheral collisions and thus allowing for a larger MB dataset to be recorded.

### Electron and photon triggers

Prompt photons probe the early stages of the HI collision as, due to their colourless nature, they are transparent to the subsequent evolution of the matter. Complementary information is provided by measuring electrons originating from \(W\) and \(Z\) boson decays. Furthermore, prompt photons or \(Z\) bosons calibrate the recoiling jet in \(\gamma\)-jet and \(Z\)-jet measurements that are essential to understand the interactions of colour charges with deconfined matter.

To improve the electron and photon trigger performance, a UE subtraction procedure [24] similar to that used in the jet trigger is used at the HLT. ### UPC triggers

Various triggers were used in the 2018 HI run to collect data with different signatures occurring in UPCs. A group of triggers was implemented to record events with the light-by-light (\(\gamma+\gamma\rightarrow\gamma+\gamma\)) scattering process [26]. The effective selection of these events significantly depends on the performance of the L1 calorimeter triggers for low-\(E_{\rm T}\) EM objects and the settings of the system were optimised for low-\(\mu\) Pb+Pb collisions. The noise settings were tuned to maintain a high efficiency for EM objects down to \(E_{\rm T}\) of 1 GeV and TE triggers with a requirement of 4 GeV for the first time, while maintaining a low rate of noise events.3 Candidate events were selected requiring a logical OR of two conditions at L1: 1) at least one EM RoI with \(E_{\rm T}>1\) GeV in coincidence with TE between 4 and 200 GeV and 2) at least two EM RoIs with \(E_{\rm T}>1\) GeV with TE below 50 GeV. The HLT requirement included the \(\Sigma E_{\rm T}^{\rm{FCal}}\) to be consistent with noise (FgapAC), and the number of hits in the pixel detector was required to be at most 15. The trigger efficiency for this process was evaluated with \(\gamma+\gamma\to e+e\) events passing an independent UPC trigger, and the resulting L1 efficiency for the pair of clusters is shown in Figure 19. The efficiency is estimated to be greater than 99%.

A dedicated trigger was introduced to collect UPC data with vector meson (VM) production, which can be used to understand the geometric structure of the nucleus. Further, a sample of UPC \(\gamma+A\rightarrow\)X events was collected with a trigger requiring a maximum TE of 200 GeV, a signal on one side and no signal on the other side in the ZDC at L1, and at least one track at the HLT.

## 6 Summary

The ATLAS trigger system was successfully operated in 2018 during proton-proton and heavy-ion data-taking. The design of the trigger menu, as discussed in this note, enables ATLAS to take data for a broad physics programme as well as for detector monitoring and calibrations. Special run conditions are covered by dedicated trigger menus. The triggers used for heavy-ion data-taking are described in detail including the data-taking strategies for different event characteristics.

The continuous effort to improve the trigger menu together with lower pileup conditions in 2018 allowed for the addition of several new triggers throughout the year targeting previously uncovered phase space. Main improvements compared to 2017 are the enhanced performance of the L1 muon trigger and the better efficiency of several HLT algorithms.

Despite challenging conditions with high luminosities and pileup, the improvements ensured smooth and efficient data-taking during 2018 and a successful conclusion of Run 2.
