## 1 Introduction

Combining the results of the ATLAS and CMS Collaborations is necessary to achieve the highest precision in top quark physics during the LHC era. Monte-Carlo (MC) modelling plays a crucial role in virtually all top quark analyses, and it is crucial to understand how to combine results given the different strategies of ATLAS and CMS in this area. Currently, ATLAS [1, 2, 3] and CMS [4] use the same generator setup - Powheg+Pythia8 - for their nominal \(t\bar{t}\) samples, but with different settings and uncertainty prescriptions, making the combination process challenging. To simplify this process, it is useful to have an agreed-upon set of parameters that both Collaborations can use to produce a shared \(t\bar{t}\) MC sample.

As both experiments currently use the next-to-leading order (NLO) in quantum chromodynamics (QCD) generator Powheg+Pythia8 for the nominal \(t\bar{t}\) modelling, "Common Settings" are proposed in this document for this generator setup. Additionally, Common Settings for the Sherpa generator are proposed and validated based upon the existing ATLAS Sherpa sample [5]. The resulting distributions of kinematic properties produced after the parton shower stage are compared between ATLAS and CMSs- well as. They are also compared to unfolded data measured by both the ATLAS and CMS Collaborations at \(\sqrt{s}=13\) TeV during Run II of the LHC. No detector simulation is applied in the studies presented here since that depends on the specific detector (ATLAS or CMS) [6].

This document presents the second iteration of these Common Settings for Powheg+Pythia8. By comparing this sample with the MC samples used by each collaboration, differences in physics distributions can be identified that help understand the correlations of systematic uncertainties due to MC modelling. This approach will also enable all future experimental results that compare multiple MC setups to include a sample produced with these Common Settings, which will make it easier to understand the trends in similar analyses with slightly different selection requirements or binning of differential distributions. Comparisons are also made with unfolded experimental data measured by both the ATLAS and CMS Collaborations at \(\sqrt{s}=13\) TeV during Run II of the LHC.

This document is structured as follows: Section 2 summarises the nominal settings of both experiments and introduces the proposed Common Settings; Section 3 presents validation plots that demonstrate that both experiments are capable of producing consistent samples with the Common Settings; Section 4provides comparisons between a sample generated with the Common Settings and the nominal samples of both ATLAS and CMS as well as experimentally measured data from a range of Run II measurements, and finally, conclusions are drawn in Section 5.

## 3 Validation

A validation is performed to confirm that both Collaborations are able to produce samples using the Common Settings with consistent results. Statistically independent samples of \(t\bar{t}\) events are produced within the ATLAS and CMS software frameworks and compared using the lepton+jets (ONELEP) mode of the MC_TTBAR Rivet[22] routine as well as the MC_FSPARTICLES Rivet routine [22] for both the Powheg + Pythia8 and Sherpa setups. Comparisons are presented for normalised differential distributions because ATLAS and CMS normalise \(t\bar{t}\) samples to the same next-to-next-to leading order theory prediction [23].

The MC_TTBAR routine selects events with exactly one lepton (electron or muon) with transverse momentum \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>30\) GeV and pseudorapidity \(|\eta|<4.2\), a minimum of four \(R=0.6\) anti-\(k_{T}\)[24, 25] jets with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>30\) GeV and \(|\eta|<4.0\), and at least 30 GeV of missing transverse energy. Jets within \(\Delta R=\sqrt{\Delta\eta^{2}+\Delta\phi^{2}}<0.3\) of the lepton are removed to avoid double counting of any energy deposits. The distributions of jet multiplicity and the scalar sum of jet \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) at this stage of the selection for Powheg + Pythia8 are shown in Figures 1(a) and 1(b), respectively. The same distributions for Sherpa are shown in Figures 3(a) and 3(b).

Subsequently, a requirement is made for exactly two of the selected jets to contain a \(b\) hadron, consistent with the two expected \(b\) quarks from the decaying top quarks. These \(b\)-jets are identified through ghost matching [26], where the \(b\) hadrons are included in the jet clustering algorithm, but with momenta manually set to a negligible value [22]. Of the non-\(b\)-jets, the pair with an invariant mass closest to \(m_{W}=80.4\) GeV is selected as the hadronically decaying \(W\)-boson candidate, and the distribution of this mass is shown in Figures 1(c) and 3(c). Two hadronically decaying top-quark candidates are built by combining the \(W\)-boson candidate with each of the \(b\) jets, and the top-quark mass distribution shown in Figures 1(d) and 3(d) is filled with the invariant mass of both of these candidates. Good agreement is observed in these and all other distributions in this routine for both setups.

Additionally, in order to closely investigate the performance of the parton shower, four distributions from the MC_FSPARTICLES routine are shown in Figures 2 and 4 for for the Powheg + Pythia8 and Sherpa setups respectively. This routine selects all final state particles with \(|\eta|<5.0\) and \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>500\) MeV. The distributions of energy, pseudo-rapidity, and particle multiplicity, shown in Figures 2(a), 2(b), and 2(c) for Powheg + Pythia8, demonstrate good agreement between the samples generated by each experiment. However, for the Powheg + Pythia8 setup, the charged particle multiplicity shown in Figure 2(d) shows a small but non-negligible slope in the ratio between the two samples. This difference is likely due to the slightly different Pythia8 versions used by the two experiments (as described in Table 1). A bug affecting the charge multiplicity in Pythia8 v240 was fixed in Pythia8 v242. This bug fix was implemented in the CMS version of Pythia8, but the fix may not be exactly the same as what is in v244 used by ATLAS. Given the excellent agreement observed in all other distributions, this small difference is deemed acceptable. The same distributions from MC_FSPARTICLES are shown for the Sherpa setup in Figure 4. These show similarly good agreement with perhaps a very slight slope in the charged multiplicity, though it is within statistical uncertainties. The validation is thus considered to demonstrate that both experiments are able to produce approximately identical samples using the Common Settings.

## 4 Comparison with data and nominal ATLAS and CMS samples

Common Powheg+Pythia8 and Sherpa samples are generated with the Common Settings detailed in Sections 2.1 and 2.2, respectively. These samples are compared to the nominal \(t\bar{t}\) samples in use by ATLAS and CMS and to LHC Run 2 data. The comparison is done for unfolded kinematic distributions measured by ATLAS and CMS based on approximately 36\(\mathrm{fb}^{-1}\) of data collected at \(\sqrt{s}=13\) TeV. Representative measurements are chosen for this comparison for the two experiments, the different \(t\bar{t}\) final states, and different object and event properties.

### ATLAS Measurements

Comparisons to ATLAS data are made for three measurements corresponding to different decay modes of the \(t\bar{t}\) system. In the first measurement, the kinematic distributions of the top quark and the \(t\bar{t}\) system, \(p_{\mathrm{T}}^{t}\), \(m^{t\bar{t}}\), and \(p_{\mathrm{T}}^{t\bar{t}}\), are measured in the boosted lepton+jets channel [27]. Events contain exactly one "dressed" lepton1 (electron or muon) with \(p_{T}>27\) GeV and \(|\eta|<2.5\). Jets, referred to here as "small-\(R\)" jets, are reconstructed with the anti-\(k_{T}\) algorithm with \(R=0.4\), and have \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>25\) GeV and \(|\eta|<2.5\). These jets are then reclustered into anti-\(k_{T}\) R=1.0 "large-\(R\)" jets, which are trimmed [28] by removing any subjets with a \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) less than 5% of the \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) of the large-\(R\) jet. The trimmed large-\(R\) jets fulfill the criteria \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>350\) GeV, \(|\eta|<2.0\), and \(120<m<220\) GeV. Further requirements are then placed on the angular separations of these objects; there must be at least one jet associated to the lepton, i.e. at least one small-\(R\) jet within \(\Delta R<2.0\) of the lepton and \(\Delta R>1.5\) of the large-\(R\) jet, and the large-\(R\) jet must be separated from the lepton by at least \(\Delta\phi>1.0\). At least one small-\(R\) jet associated to the lepton, or reclustered into the large-\(R\) jet, must be ghost-matched to a \(b\) hadron. Finally, the missing transverse energy is required to be \(>20\) GeV, and the sum of missing transverse energy and transverse mass of the \(W\) boson must be \(>60\) GeV. Figures 5, 6, and 7 show distributions from this measurement.

Footnote 1: A lepton is â€œdressedâ€ by adding the momenta of all photons within a cone of \(\Delta R=0.1\) around the lepton to account for final state radiation.

The second ATLAS measurement presents the same three distributions using events in the all-hadronic boosted channel [29]. Events contain no dressed electrons or muons with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>25\) GeV and \(|\eta|<2.5\), at least one large-\(R\) jet with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>500\) GeV and \(|\eta|<2.0\), an additional large-\(R\) jet with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>350\) GeV and the same \(\eta\) requirement, and at least two small-\(R\) jets with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>25\) GeV and \(|\eta|<2.5\). Both large-\(R\) jets have \(m=172.5\pm 50\) GeV and are ghost-matched to a \(b\) hadron. The distributions from this measurement are shown in Figures 5, 6, and 7.

The final measurement uses a dilepton selection [30] with exactly one dressed electron and one dressed muon, each with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>20\) GeV and \(|\eta|<2.5\). No further requirements are made on the jets or other objects in the event. The azimuthal separation between the two leptons, \(\Delta\phi^{e\mu}\), is shown in Figure 10.

### CMS Measurements

Comparisons with CMS data are made for three measurements in the lepton+jets channel. The first measurement presents kinematic quantities (\(p_{\mathrm{T}}^{t}\), \(m^{t\bar{t}}\), and \(p_{\mathrm{T}}^{t\bar{t}}\)) related to the \(t\bar{t}\) system [31]. Events contain exactly one dressed lepton (electron or muon) with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>30\) GeV and \(|\eta|<2.4\), with a veto applied on any events containing an additional electron or muon with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>15\) GeV and the same \(\eta\) requirement. Atleast four small-\(R\) (\(R=0.4\)) anti-\(k_{\mathrm{T}}\) jets with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>25\) GeV and \(|\eta|<2.4\) are required, at least two of which are ghost-matched \(b\) hadrons. The hadronic top quark is resolved, i.e. it is reconstructed from three small-\(R\) jets. The \(t\bar{t}\) system is reconstructed by finding the event permutation which minimises the mass variable

\[\left[M(p_{\nu}+p_{l}+p_{b_{l}})-m_{t}\right]^{2}+\left[M(p_{j_{\nu 1}}+p_{j_{\nu 2 }}-m_{W}\right]^{2}+\left[M(p_{j_{\nu 1}}+p_{j_{\nu 2}}+p_{b_{h}})-m_{t}\right]^{2}, \tag{1}\]

where \(m_{t}=172.5\) GeV, \(m_{W}=80.4\) GeV, \(p_{\nu}\) is the four-vector sum of all neutrinos, and \(p_{b_{l},h}\), \(p_{j_{\nu 1,2}}\) and \(p_{l}\) are the four-momenta of the \(b\)-jet candidates, the light jet candidates, and the lepton, respectively. The distributions shown in Figures 5, 5, 6, and 7 are from this measurement.

The second measurement gives kinematic distributions of the event: the jet multiplicity (\(N_{\mathrm{jet}}\)) and the scalar sum of the \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) of all jets in the event (\(H_{\mathrm{T}}\)) [32]. The event selection is similar to the other CMS lepton+jets measurement [31], with the exceptions that the leptons must have \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>26\) GeV, and the jet requirement is to have at least three jets with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>30\) GeV and at least one further jet with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>20\) GeV. The \(N_{\mathrm{jet}}\) and \(H_{\mathrm{T}}\) distributions from this measurement are shown in Figure 8.

Finally, comparisons are made with a measurement of jet substructure observables in boosted hadronic top-quark decays [33]. The same single lepton requirement is made lepton selection is applied as in the previous measurement [32], while at least four anti-\(k_{\mathrm{T}}\) small-\(R\) jets are required with \(p_{\mathrm{T}}>30\) GeV and \(|\eta|<2.5\). Two of these jets are ghost-matched to a \(b\) hadron, and at least one pair of the remaining light jets has an invariant mass within \(\pm 15\) GeV of \(m_{W}=80.4\) GeV. Figure 9 contains two distributions from this measurement, that of N-subjettiness variables [34]\(\tau_{21}\) and \(\tau_{32}\) for all jets in the event.

### Comparisons

Figure 5 shows that the top \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) is modelled similarly between ATLAS and the Powheg+Pythia8 Common Settings v2, with a slightly softer distribution for CMS. The common Sherpa settings produce significantly softer \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) distributions, which also agree best with the ATLAS and CMS data.

Figure 6 shows that the \(p_{\mathrm{T}}\) distribution of the \(t\bar{t}\) system is modelled similarly by all generators and between ATLAS and CMS for boosted events. However, this is not the case for the resolved selection shown in Figure 6, which is more sensitive to the detailed modelling of the individual small-\(R\) jets in these resolved events. Here, Sherpa differs from the Powheg+Pythia8 distributions and agrees better with data.

Figure 7 shows a similar behavior for the mass of the \(t\bar{t}\) system. For boosted events, all generators predict similar distributions, while for resolved events with small-\(R\) jets, neither Powheg+Pythia8 nor Sherpa model the distribution this observable well at low mass, and Powheg+Pythia8 agrees better with data at high mass, especially the CMS sample, but also the Common Settings v2.

Figure 8 shows that there are also differences between the generators for the event energy and jet multiplicity, two distributions observables that are difficult to model accurately, and even more difficult to do so simultaneously for both distributions observables. The ATLAS and CMS distributions are very similar, and notably different from the distributions for the Common Settings v2, which give show the worst agreement with data for \(H_{\mathrm{T}}\), and the best agreement with data for \(N_{\mathrm{jets}}\). The Common Sherpa settings produce excellent agreement for \(H_{\mathrm{T}}\), but not for \(N_{\mathrm{jets}}\).

The jet substructure variables shown in Figure 9 are not modelled very well by any of the four generators, though Sherpa comes closest. ## 5 Summary

The second iteration of Common ATLAS + CMS Settings for \(t\bar{t}\) Powheg+Pythia8 simulation has been presented, together with a first common Sherpa setup. The Powheg+Pythia8 settings are chosen to better describe data-form a consistent set compared to v1 [37]. The agreement of the Common Settings v2 sample with data is similar to that of the default ATLAS and CMS Powheg+Pythia8 samples. The Sherpa \(t\bar{t}\) sample is the first such sample for CMS, and with common Settings (identical to existing ATLAS settings), improved agreement with unfolded \(t\bar{t}\) data is observed. It has been validated that each experimental-Collaboration can produce approximately identical samples for each setup. Comparisons have been made to unfolded data from both ATLAS and CMS for a large range of final states and kinematic distributions. Future ATLAS +CMS-and CMS measurements and combinations can benefit from this common point of comparison. Detailed studies of modeling and systematic uncertainties that are consistent between the two Collaborations are now possible. Common Settings that are fine-tuned to both ATLAS and CMS data could become part of the default set of samples in the future, thereby enabling sharing of computing resources for both current and future generators.
