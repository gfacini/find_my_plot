**Search for the standard model Higgs boson**

**in the \(\ttbar\ttbar\ttbar\tt\), \(\tt H\to WW^{(*)}\) channel**

J. Leveque, J. B. de Vivie, V. Kostiouk hine, A. Rozanov

_Abstract_

A new channel, \(\ttbar\ttttbar\tt H\), \(\tt H\to WW^{(*)}\), giving a direct access to the top quark Yukawa coupling for a Higgs boson in the intermediate mass range [120-240] \(\rm GeV/c^{2}\) is studied. In spite of a low signal rate and the absence of a reconstructed mass peak, a reasonable significance is obtained by a large removal of the background. In the most favorable case \(m_{\rm H}\)= 160 \(\rm GeV/c^{2}\), the combination of two different final states allows to measure the cross section with a relative statistical error around 26% for an integrated luminosity L of 30 fb\({}^{-1}\), and less than 15% after three years of data taking at high luminosity, corresponding to L = 300 fb\({}^{-1}\).

Introduction

The discovery of the standard model Higgs boson is expected at LHC in less than one year at low luminosity if its mass (\(m_{\rm H}\)) is above \(130\leavevmode\nobreak\ {\rm GeV}/c^{2}\) by using the _golden mode_\({\rm H}\to{\rm ZZ}^{(*)}\to 4\ell\) and in less than two years by combining ATLAS and CMS at masses around \(115\leavevmode\nobreak\ {\rm GeV}/c^{2}\), where the processes \({\rm pp}\to({\rm H}\to\gamma\gamma)\) and \({\rm pp}\to{\rm t}\overline{\rm t}\,({\rm H}\to{\rm b}\overline{\rm b})\) have to be considered [1]. However, measuring the parameters of the Higgs sector is much more difficult and any process which could be observed above the standard model background should be used, even if its rate is low.

In this note, a study of the process \({\rm pp}\to{\rm t}\overline{\rm t}\,({\rm H}\to{\rm WW}^{(*)})\) is presented. Although the \({\rm t}\overline{\rm t}\,{\rm H}\) cross section is rather low and decreases rapidly with \(m_{\rm H}\), it is compensated by the increase of the \({\rm H}\to{\rm WW}^{(*)}\) branching ratio as illustrated in Fig. 1, leading to a reasonable number of events for \(m_{\rm H}\in[120,240]\leavevmode\nobreak\ {\rm GeV}/c^{2}\).

This process can give rise to spectacular final states [2], with many jets and many leptons. Assuming that the background is well estimated, it may give a direct access to the top quark Yukawa coupling \(\lambda_{t}\), for \(m_{\rm H}\leq 240\leavevmode\nobreak\ {\rm GeV}/c^{2}\). However, since at least two leptons are required to suppress the background, two neutrinos are present in the final state and no mass peak can be reconstructed in a simple way.

The Yukawa coupling of the top quark may be determined indirectly through the measurement of the \({\rm gg}\to{\rm H}\) process. Because this is a loop induced process, the deleterious contributions of new heavy colored particles such as quarks from a fourth heavy family [3] or squarks strongly coupled to the Higgs boson could falsify this determination. A comparison of this indirect measurement with a direct one through the \({\rm t}\overline{\rm t}\,{\rm H}\) process could give an insight to new physics. Moreover, by studying simultaneously the \({\rm gg}\to{\rm H}\ \to\ {\rm WW}^{(*)}\) and the \({\rm t}\overline{\rm t}\,({\rm H}\to{\rm WW}^{(*)})\) processes, many systematic uncertainties in the decay of the

In Sections 2 to 4 of this note, a complete analysis of the different possible signal final states is performed for an integrated luminosity of 30 fb\({}^{-1}\). Finally, our results are compared with a similar analysis, recently performed at the parton level by F. Maltoni _et al._ ([4]) in Section 7.

The \(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}\mathrm{b}\overline{\mathrm{b}}\) (\(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}\to 1\ell,2\ell\)) 2, \(\mathrm{Z}\mathrm{b}\overline{\mathrm{b}}\) (\(\mathrm{Z}\to\ell\ell\)) and \(\mathrm{W}\mathrm{b}\overline{\mathrm{b}}\) (\(\mathrm{W}\to\ell\nu\)) events have been generated with the matrix element implementation in PYTHIA 6.157. For \(\mathrm{Z}+\mathrm{jets}\), WW, WZ and ZZ, only final states with at least one gauge boson decaying into leptons have been simulated. The \(\mathrm{t}\mathrm{t}\mathrm{Z}(\gamma^{*})\) (\(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}\to 1\ell,2\ell\); \(\mathrm{Z}\to\ell\ell\), \(\mathrm{b}\overline{\mathrm{b}}\), \(\nu\nu\)) and \(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}\mathrm{W}\) (\(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}\to 1\ell,2\ell\); \(\mathrm{W}\to\ell\nu\)) background processes have been generated with the AcerMC generator [8], interfaced with PYTHIA 6.2. ### Basic selection

At least two leptons and six jets with \(p_{T}>15\) GeV /c and \(|\eta|<2.5\) are required. A lepton identification and reconstruction efficiency of 0.9 is assumed. In the dominant background processes \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\) (2\(\ell\)) and Z + jets, all light jets come from FSR or ISR. Fig. 4 illustrates the pseudo-rapidity of light jets in \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\)H, \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\) (2\(\ell\)) and Z + jets. A cut on \(|\eta|_{\mathrm{jet}}\) at 2.5 allows to suppress a fraction of the ISR contribution, and decrease the number of jets in background events.

In signal events, two of the jets should be tagged as b-jets. A loose b-tag with efficiency \(\epsilon_{\mathrm{b}}=80\%\) and rejections \(R_{udsg}=15,\ R_{\mathrm{c}}=2\) is applied, in order to suppress the Z+jets events and keep a large fraction of the signal. Using the standard b-tag efficiency and rejection seems not to be optimal since the dominant background processes contain genuine b jets, as for the signal.

Table 3 presents the cross section for the different background processes remaining after this basic selection. The dominant ones are \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\) (74.6%), \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\)Z (9.9%), \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\)bb (8.6%) and Z+jets (5.5%). In the dominant background \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\)(2\(\ell\)), the two leading leptons have opposite charges. Requiring that the two most energetic leptons have the same charge reduces in a drastic way the \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\)(2\(\ell\)) contribution3, from 88.9 fb to 0.2 fb. The Z+jets background becomes negligible. Half of the signal and \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\)(1\(\ell\)) background events remain. In these \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\)(1\(\ell\))

### Rejection of leptons from semi-leptonic b-decays

The properties of b-leptons can be exploit to further reduce the \(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}(1\ell)\) background process. Due to the long lifetime of b-hadrons, b-leptons should have a large transverse impact parameter \(|\mathrm{d}_{0}|\) in comparison with prompt leptons coming from W bosons. This is illustrated in Fig. 5. A smearing for the beam spot size has been added to the default ATLFAST smearing, to obtain a resolution in agreement with the TDR values. Rejecting events with large \(|\mathrm{d}_{0}|\) could reduce the b-lepton contribution. However, no improvement has been observed when cutting on this variable for the 2L final state; it will only be used for the 3L channel.

In the selected events, the b-leptons are more often electrons than muons. This feature is due to the isolation criteria used in ATLFAST: first, the distance between the electron cluster and the other calorimetric clusters should be greater than 0.4. But if a particle (e.g. a pion) is very close to the electron, it is added in the same cluster, and this electron is considered as isolated. To identify these leptons, the energy deposition in calorimeter cells in a cone of size \(\Delta\mathrm{R}=0.2\) around the electron track is compared to the momentum of the electron, and the difference is required to be less than 10 GeV. The distribution is shown in Fig. 6 (left) for W-leptons and b-leptons. This variable discriminates b-leptons and W-leptons; however since the shape of the electromagnetic shower is not taken into account in ATLFAST, more full simulation studies are needed before using a tighter cut.

Therefore, additional cuts on more reliable quantities are applied. A track isolation [10] is added to the calorimeter isolation. The distribution of the \(p_{T}\) of the most energetic track in a cone of size \(\Delta\mathrm{R}=0.2\) around the lepton is shown in Fig. 6 (right) for W-leptons and b-leptons. The maximum value of this variable (\(p_{Tr}^{max}\)) for the two leptons is required to be less than 1.5 GeV/\(c\). This isolation criterion suppresses 75% of the \(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}\) background and 10% of the signal.

### \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}Z/t\overline{t}W}\) backgrounds

In the \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}Z}\) and \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}W}\) events which are contributing to the background, one lepton comes from a top decay and the other from the Z or W decay. The \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}Z}\) background can be suppressed by applying a veto on a third isolated lepton. Moreover, in a small part of the remaining events, a pair of opposite sign and same flavor leptons is found. One of these leptons (with \(p_{T}~{}>\) 6 GeV/\(c\) and \(|\eta|<2.5\)) is not isolated 4. Events where the lepton invariant mass is in the range \([75,100]\) GeV/\(c^{2}\) are rejected. The \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}Z}\) contribution decreases by 33 %.

### The \(\ttbar\tt Wq\) process

From the above results, it can be seen that the \(\qq\,^{\prime}\to\tt\bar Wq\) process with two additional jets originating from radiation represents \(\sim 30\) % of the total background. The process \(\qq\to\tt\bar Wq\,^{\prime}\) is similar to the previous one and could also contribute to the background. Some Feynman diagrams are shown in Fig. 11.

Although it is a higher order process in gauge couplings, an enhancement can be expected due to a high gluon flux in the proton (compared to the antiquark one) and to one additional parton in the final state.

This process has been estimated with the CompHEP generator. There are 74 diagrams which contribute to its matrix element. The differential cross section has a peak when the light quark scatters in the forward direction. This peak is difficult to integrate; moreover for small \(p_{T}\) a problem of double counting with the structure function evolution arises. Nevertheless, with \(p_{T}(\qq\,^{\prime})\geq 10\) GeV/\(c\), the matrix element can be integrated and gives a total cross section \(\sim 550\) fb. The matrix element includes some diagrams which can be described as a convolution of the \(\tt\bar Wq\) matrix element with a gluon splitting \(\qq\to\qq\) in the initial state. These diagrams are intrinsically present in the events produced by AcerMC+PYTHIA (with ISR on) although they are not included in the LO \(\tt\bar Wq\) cross section. To avoid double counting, events generated with AcerMC where one of the incoming partons was produced in a gluon splitting are flagged and removed from the final results (At this stage of the analysis, this corresponds to 3 events).

The \(\tt\bar Wq\) events generated by CompHEP have been processed with PYTHIA to obtain the complete final state (FSR, hadronization), and reconstructed with ATLFAST. This gives an additional contribution of 19.7 background events for the 2L channel.

### Mass reconstruction : constraint fit

For further background reduction, the reconstruction of the top quarks and Higgs boson could certainly be helpful. This reconstruction is impossible in the case of the 3L final state, because too many variables are involved with the three neutrinos. But in case of the 2L final state, a kinematic fit shows some sensitivity to the Higgs boson mass and the possibility to discriminate the process from the background.

Figure 11: Examples of Feynman diagrams for the \(\tt\bar Wq\) process.

The fit procedure aims at completely reconstructing the \(\mathrm{t}(\rightarrow\mathrm{jjb})\)\(\mathrm{t}(\rightarrow\ell\nu\mathrm{b})\)\(\mathrm{H}(\rightarrow\mathrm{WW}\rightarrow\mathrm{jj}\ell\nu)\) system with five constraints:

* \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{jj}}=m_{\mathrm{\,W}}\)\(\mathrm{(t}\rightarrow\mathrm{q}\,\overline{\mathrm{q}}\,^{\prime}\mathrm{b})\)
* \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{l}\nu}=m_{\mathrm{\,W}}\)\(\mathrm{(t}\rightarrow\ell\nu\mathrm{b})\)
* \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{j}\mathrm{b}}=m_{\mathrm{\,t}}\)\(\mathrm{(t}\rightarrow\mathrm{q}\,\overline{\mathrm{q}}\,^{\prime}\mathrm{b})\)
* \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{l}\nu\mathrm{b}}=m_{\mathrm{\,t}}\)\(\mathrm{(t}\rightarrow\ell\nu\mathrm{b})\)
* \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{j}\mathrm{j}}=m_{\mathrm{\,W}}\) and \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{l}\nu}\leq m_{\mathrm{\,W}}\) or \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{j}\mathrm{j}}\leq m_{\mathrm{\,W}}\) and \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{l}\nu}=m_{\mathrm{\,W}}\)\(\mathrm{(H}\rightarrow\mathrm{WW}^{(*)})\)

The exact type of the last constraint was selected for each combination based on the \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{jj}}\) invariant mass from Higgs boson decay. For \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{jj}}>62\)\(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2}\)\(\mathrm{(M}_{\mathrm{jj}}<62\)\(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2})\), the \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{jj}}=m_{\mathrm{\,W}}\)\(\mathrm{(M}_{\mathrm{l}\nu}=m_{\mathrm{\,W}})\) constraint was selected The fit returns improved jet energies together with the two neutrino four-momenta. Lepton four-momenta are considered as being precisely measured and are not modified in the fit.

Due to presence of FSR and limited detector acceptance, a reconstructed jets+leptons configuration may deviate from the initial parton one. In order to reduce the influence of the deviation on the accuracy of the reconstruction, a robust fitting functional is used:

\[\chi^{2}=\sum_{i=1}^{6}\Psi(E_{i}^{0}-E_{i}^{fit})+\Psi(P_{\nu_{1}}^{x}+P_{\nu _{2}}^{x}-P_{miss}^{x})+\Psi(P_{\nu_{1}}^{y}+P_{\nu_{2}}^{y}-P_{miss}^{y}), \tag{1}\]

where \(\Psi(x)\) is a modification of the usual \(\chi^{2}(x)=x^{2}\) term (details can be found in Ref. [11]). A special program for nonlinear minimization with constraints [12] is used to find a minimum of the functional.

Signal events may have in general more than six jets and two leptons. From all possible combinations \(\mathrm{jjb}\,(\mathrm{top})+\ell\mathrm{b}\,(\mathrm{top})+\mathrm{jj}\ell( \mathrm{Higgs})\), only "good" ones are kept for the constraint reconstruction. A "good" combination has to satisfy the following conditions:

* \(\mathrm{|M}_{\mathrm{jj}}-m_{\mathrm{\,W}}|<25\)\(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2}\)\(\mathrm{(t}\rightarrow\mathrm{q}\,\overline{\mathrm{q}}\,^{\prime}\mathrm{b})\)
* \(\mathrm{|M}_{\mathrm{jjb}}-m_{\mathrm{\,t}}|<30\)\(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2}\)\(\mathrm{(t}\rightarrow\mathrm{q}\,\overline{\mathrm{q}}\,^{\prime}\mathrm{b})\)
* \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{fb}}<180\)\(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2}\)\(\mathrm{(t}\rightarrow\ell\nu\mathrm{b})\)
* \(\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{jj}}<110\)\(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2}\)\(\mathrm{(H}\rightarrow\mathrm{q}\,\overline{\mathrm{q}}\,^{\prime}\ell\nu)\)

Because of the huge number of "good" combinations for a single event (this number may reach 100), it is likely that the minimum \(\chi^{2}\) criterion selects a combinatorial background solution. The minimum invariant mass \(\mathrm{M}(\mathrm{jj}\ell\nu_{\mathrm{ec}})\) is a better variable for background rejection. For signal events, this mass should correspond to the Higgs boson mass and is relatively small. In background events, it is the mass of a random set \(\mathrm{jets}+\mathrm{lepton}+\nu\) selected from a final state with a large total mass (\(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}\mathrm{W}(\mathrm{q})\), \(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}\mathrm{Z}\)) and is therefore not expected to be small. Typical distributions of this variable for signal and background events are shown in Fig.12.

An additional constraint on the Higgs boson mass ( measured in others channels) could improved the rejection power of the fit.

### Summary

After all the cuts shown in Table 4 have been applied, the number of \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}W}\) events from AcerMC is 9.4 (when the overlap with CompHEP has been removed). The contribution of the \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}Wq}\) process amounts to 19.7 events, leading to a total background level of 58.0 events. The expectation from a 160 \(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2}\) Higgs boson is 30.6 events.

A cut on the minimum reconstructed mass is then applied: \(\mathrm{M}(\mathrm{jj}\ell\nu_{\mathrm{rec}})\leq 200\)\(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2}\) (if no good combination is found during the fit, the event is rejected). It allows to suppress a large fraction of the background as shown in Table 5.

The maximum is reached at 160 \(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2}\), where 21.1\(\pm\)0.6 signal events are expected. The significances of the signal shown in Fig. 13 is computed according to Poisson statistic and do not include the systematic uncertainties.

## 4 The three lepton and four jet final state

The rate of this final state is very similar to the one of the 2L final state when the Like Sign cut is applied. The presence of three hard leptons gives a very clean signature with few reducible background events expected. In the following, all numbers of events are given for an integrated luminosity of \(30\) fb\({}^{-1}\). A factor of 0.9 has been applied for each lepton to take into account the reconstruction and identification efficiency.

The basic selection is very simple: at least three leptons and four jets with \(|\eta|<2.5\) and \(p_{T}>15\) GeV/\(c\) have to be reconstructed. As for the 2L final state, two of the jets must be loosely b-tagged. When this selection is applied, \(\sim 184\) events are expected from the standard model background, the majority of them coming from the \(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}(1\ell)\mathrm{Z}\) (126 events) and the \(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}(2\ell)\) (32 events) processes; the number of signal events varies between 5.9 and 24.7 for \(m_{\mathrm{H}}\in[120,240]\) GeV/\(c^{2}\).

As explained in Section 3, in reducible background events, originating from the \(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}\), \(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{b}}\overline{\mathrm{b}}\), and gauge boson production processes, at least one of the leptons comes from the semileptonic decay of a b-hadron. These events are further suppressed by applying isolation cuts, cuts on impact parameters and tightening the cut on the lepton \(p_{T}\). A cut on \(p_{T}^{max}\) (defined as in Section 3) at 2 GeV/\(c\) has been applied. The maximal lepton transverse impact parameter is required to be below \(150\)\(\mu m\). Finally, \({p_{T}}^{\ell_{3}}>20\) GeV/\(c\), \({p_{T}}^{\ell_{2}}>25\) GeV/\(c\) and \({p_{T}}^{\ell_{1}}>30\) GeV/\(c\) if it is an electron (the cut for muons is left unchanged at 15 GeV/\(c\)), where \({p_{T}}^{\ell_{1}},{p_{T}}^{\ell_{2}}\) and \({p_{T}}^{\ell_{3}}\) are the \(p_{T}\) of the most, second most and third most energetic leptons in the event. Table 6 shows the background composition after that selection. The total background amounts to \(\sim 106\) expected events, among which \(\sim 94\) events come from the \(\mathrm{t}\overline{\mathrm{t}}\)(Z\(\rightarrow\ell^{+}\ell^{-}\)) process. The \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\)Z process is further suppressed in two ways. A fraction of the \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\)(2\(\ell\))Z events contains four well isolated leptons. As for the 2L final state, some variables may still be exploited to disentangle background and signal events. However, the signal receives contribution from two distinct final states (depending on whether the Higgs boson or the \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\) system decay into two leptons), and more variables have to be considered. For instance, to fully make use of the spin correlation between both W from the Higgs boson decay, the minimum distance between one lepton and a jet or between two leptons should be used. This dilutes the discriminating power of such variable and given the already small number of signal events, almost inevitably leads to multivariate techniques. This has not been attempted for the time being.

## 7 Comparison with an alternative analysis

Recently, Maltoni _et al._([4]) have performed a similar analysis of the \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}H,H\to WW^{(*)}}\) process at the parton level. For a luminosity of 300 fb\({}^{-1}\), the Yukawa coupling of the top quark is estimated to be measured with a statistical uncertainty of 16%, 8%, 12% for \(m_{H}\)= 130, 160, 190 \(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2}\) respectively.

Here, the same analysis is repeated with full hadronization and fast simulation of the ATLAS detector, in order to check the results and to determine the detector effects. The acceptance cuts and detection efficiencies applied are shown in Table 10. The background processes and selection criteria for each channel are presented separately below.

### Two lepton channel

The background processes considered by Maltoni _et al._ for this channel are \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}\ell^{+}\ell^{-}}\), \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}W}\), \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}W^{+}W^{-}}\) and \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}t\overline{t}}\). The \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}}\) background is not taken into account. To simulate Maltoni's analysis, very simple cuts are used: exactly two leptons with the same charge and two b-jets are required. The tight cut on the b-jet number is used against the \(\mathrm{t\overline{t}t\overline{t}}\) background. Events with at least four light jets are accepted, and at least two pairs of these jets should have a mass in the range \(m_{W}\pm\) 25 \(\mathrm{GeV}/c^{2}\).

## 8 Conclusion

This analysis demonstrates that a direct measurement of the top quark Yukawa coupling seems feasible for a Higgs boson with a mass in the range [130-220] GeV/\(c^{2}\), through the study of the t\(\overline{\rm t}\)(H\(\rightarrow\) WW\({}^{(*)}\)) final state. After three years of high luminosity data taking, a statistical precision \(\frac{\Delta\lambda_{t}}{\lambda_{t}}\sim 0.07\) could be achieved, by combining the two and three lepton channels. However, it should be noted that these results are certainly too optimistic : the background estimation suffers from large uncertainties, especially the t\(\overline{\rm t}\)W(q), t\(\overline{\rm t}\)Z and t\(\overline{\rm t}\)t\(\overline{\rm t}\) processes, and deserves more theoretical and Monte Carlo studies. In addition, the systematic uncertainties are expected to be high, and would also require further investigations.
