## 1 Introduction

The numbers describing the performance of the ATLAS detector for discovery of the intermediate mass Higgs have been updated for the open presentation of the ATLAS LOI. This note describes the changes in the analysis leading to the results that were presented. First, an unbiased generation of the top background was used and the old values of the top backgrounds were replaced. Second, the isolation numbers were revised in light of better fragmentation functions for the top quark. In the course of this update, some additional studies were conducted to understand the variations of the background and signal with some of the cuts.

## 2 Update of Top Background

There are two reasons to change the top background calculation. First, a bug was discovered in the value for the \(\sigma\times BR\) in Table 1 of the note on the intermediate mass Higgs [1]. The value given there was \(\sigma\times BR=1.62\) pb. The correct value is \(\sigma\times BR=0.41\) pb. This is obtained by taking the total cross section of 3300 pb and multiplying by the squares of each of the W and b leptonic branching ratios: \(0.41=(0.11)^{2}\times(0.10)^{2}\times 3300\). It is important to note that for the LOI, an additional step was taken. The background calculated from top decays to a single lepton (_either_ or \(\mu\)) was multiplied by a factor of four to obtain the decay to any lepton. It turns out that this factor is correct, but not trivially so! This is shown in Tab. 1 where all possible combinations of the leptons from the top and bottom quarks are listed. For each combination, a weight is given to indicate the number of ways one can form two Z's from these leptons. Note that the total number of ways to form two Z's is 8 and that this is four times the total number of ways to form two Z's from just one type of lepton. Hence, ignoring kinematics, the factor of four is correct.

Second, top was generated in an unbiased way, without forcing any semileptonic decays, thus taking into account leptons from cascade decays, which form a significant fraction of the background [2]. The effect of this bias can be seen in fig 1 where threeways of generating top are compared. The largest background values come from unbiased top and can be seen to diverge from the approximation used by Nisati [6] (middle values), which forced W leptonic decays, and that used in the LoI, which forced both W and b leptonic decays. It can be seen that there is much more sensitivity to background as one lowers the lowest \(p_{t}\) cut and takes the full top calculation into account. The ratio of the backgrounds is shown as a function of the \(p_{T}\) cut in fig. 2. For the cuts chosen in the LoI (\(p_{T}>10\) GeV), the increase in top background is \(\sim 3\), still not enough to compensate for the original normalisation error.

The parameterization of the 4-lepton mass distribution after cuts, for the top background, was obtained by fitting the distribution shown in fig. 3 and using the fit only in the region of interest (m\({}_{t\mu t}\) =100 to 200 GeV/c\({}^{2}\)). Figure 4 compares the fitted curves for the three top calculations. The approximate top and estimate used in the LOI differ exactly by a factor of four. The full calculation is the one used presently (within it's limited statistics) [3]. A large-statistics sample of unbiased top events is being generated now in Oxford with a modified top fragmentation and the study of this sample will be the subject of a future note [2].

## 3 Update of the Lepton Isolation Studies

The rejection of the dominant top background, using isolation cuts, has been updated by the completion of two recent studies :

1. A more appropriate definition of top quark fragmentation for the large top quark masses usually assumed in these studies [4].
2. An analysis of the complete statistics of fully GEANT simulated 4-lepton events from top production and \(Zb\bar{b}\) production, combined with a study of lepton isolation cut efficiency on fully GEANT simulated Higgs signal events (\(m_{H}=150\) GeV) overlaid with pile-up from an average of 40 minimum bias pile-up events [5].

We briefly describe the main results from these studies below. A more detailed description will be available in the near future in separate notes.

The top quark fragmentation in PYTHIA had been originally implemented for top quark masses below the W-mass. A consequence of this was that b-quarks from top decay do not radiate gluons. A fix for this was used in top quark studies for Aachen, but studies show that this fix is not good enough. Recently T. Sjostrand has implemented a fix, which allows one to decay top quarks in the (assumed) most reasonable way for top quark masses larger than \(\sim\) 100 GeV/c\({}^{2}\). Fig. 5 shows the rejection of a particle-level isolation cut against leptons with \(p_{T}>\) 10 GeV/c as a function of the b-quark transverse momentum for b-quarks from top events produced with the old fragmentation (full lines), from top events produced with the new (softer) top fragmentation (dashed lines) and from \(Zb\bar{b}\) events, where b-quarks were fragmented with the Lund fragmentation function (dotted lines). For values of the b-quark transverse momentum below \(\sim\) 60 GeV/c, there is now much better agreement between the b-quarks from top decay and from \(Zb\bar{b}\) production. This is what would be expected for b-quarks with the same fragmentation properties.

This study also allowed us to draw firmer conclusions on the acceptance for 4-lepton events from top production, before and after isolation. The softer b-quark fragmentation now used results in a softer lepton \(p_{T}\)-spectrum and therefore in an acceptance lower by 38% than the one obtained for the events used in the LoI. However, the softer fragmentation implies that isolation cuts are less effective by a factor \(\sim\) 3 when applied to all four leptons. The results obtained for the rejection of top background events using isolation cuts, as studied with the old fragmentation, have therefore been renormalised to take into account these effects (see below).

We believe that an accurate estimate of the absolute value of the rejection obtainable against 4-lepton events containing non-isolated leptons can only be reliably estimated through a realistic simulation of the detector. In fact, the effect of pile-up on the efficiency for the Higgs signal also warrants a full detector simulation.

For these reasons, samples of 4-electron events from top quark production (old fragmentation), \(Zb\bar{b}\) production (matrix element generation), and Higgs decay (\(m_{H}\) = 150 GeV) have been run over the past months through a full simulation of the ATLAS detector (concept B). The Higgs signal events were overlaid with variable amounts of minimum bias events to study the effect of pile-up on the efficiency of the chosen isolation cuts. For the moment we have chosen to study only 4-electron events which are the most difficult ones to treat with calorimeter isolation cuts because of the finite size of the electron shower itself.

The cuts studied below always correspond to an isolation area of size 0.4 \(\times\) 0.4 in \(\eta\times\phi\) space. The events were selected according to the same cuts as in the LoI and were then examined in terms of two isolation variables :

1. Calorimeter isolation over the isolation area described above minus the central 25 electromagnetic cells, which correspond to an area of size 0.1 \(\times\) 0.1 in \(\eta\times\phi\) space. Figure 6 shows the rejection of the dominant 4-lepton background from top production as a function of the efficiency for the Higgs signal in the presence of pile-up (an average of 40 minimum bias events). The results are shown for cuts applied to the two electrons, which do not ccrrrespond to the "real Z-candidate", and also for cuts applied to all four electrons. As expected for non-resonant 4-lepton background events, the rejection, for a given Higgs signal efficiency, is much better when applied to all four electrons.
2. Tracking isolation, requiring that no track with transverse momentum above a given threshold is found in the isolation cone. Since separate studies have demonstrated that the track reconstruction efficiency should be close to 99% (!) down to \(p_{\rm T}\sim 1\) GeV, even in the presence of pile-up, the generated tracks were used in the absence of a fully efficient global pattern recognition program at present. In a similar way to the calorimeter isolation, fig. 7 shows the performance of the tracking isolation criterion alone. Similar conclusions can be drawn, with the noticeable difference that significant rejection from tracking isolation can be obtained even for high values of the Higgs signal efficiency.

These two somewhat complementary isolation cuts can be combined to yield an optimum rejection for a given efficiency. Fig. 8 shows the efficiency, for the Higgs signal, of isolation cuts as a function of pile-up, separately for the calorimeter, tracking and combined isolation cuts. For a given type of cut, the cut values are chosen in such a way as to maintain a constant rejection for each value of pile-up. The tracking isolation, as expected, is more sensitive to pile-up than the calorimeter isolation.

With combined cuts of 12 GeV on the calorimeter isolation and 2.5 GeV on the tracking isolation, applied to all four leptons, a rejection of \(\sim 60\) can be achieved against the top background, for a Higgs signal efficiency of \(\sim 50\%\). Because these results were obtained with the old top fragmentation, we reduce this value to 30 in the following. The rejection against \(Zb\bar{b}\) events for the same cuts is \(\sim 15\).
