# Reconstruction of the Jet Energy

Taking Into Account Parameter Correlations

Regis Lefevre and Claudio Santoni

###### Abstract

Different weighting procedure were proposed to improve the determination of the jet energy in the ATLAS calorimeters. In this note a method to improve the parameter determination is discussed. It is based on the Maximum Likelihood principle and allows to take into account correlations. It was applied to simulated jets and a significant improvement was obtained on the linearity of the reconstructed energy.

###### Abstract

The _ATLAS_ calorimeters are not compensating. To improve the linearity and minimise the width of the total energy distribution an offline calibration procedure can be applied [1, 2]. It consists of two steps.

_Step 1_

The reconstructed energy of the jet \(k\) produced by a quark of energy \(E_{0}\) can be expressed by

\[E_{rec}^{k}=f\Big{(}\overline{E};\overline{a}\Big{)}\qquad\quad(1)\]

where \(\overline{E}\) is the energy deposited in the samplings or in the cells of the calorimeters associated to the jet. It was obtained using the electron calibration constants. The values of the parameters \(\overline{a}\equiv a_{l}\big{(}l=1,...,L\big{)}\) are determined requiring the minimum for the function

\[\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}\!\Big{(}E_{rec}^{k}-E_{kin}^{k}\Big{)}^{2}+\alpha\sum \limits_{k=1}^{n}\!\Big{(}E_{rec}^{k}-E_{kin}^{k}\Big{)}\,\qquad(2)\]

where \(n\) is the number of simulated jets. The total energy of the particles associated to the jet \(E_{kin}^{k}\) is assumed to be known. The simultaneous minimisation with respect to \(\alpha\), the Lagrange parameter, constraints the mean reconstructed energy to the value of \(\Big{\langle}E_{kin}^{k}\Big{\rangle}\).

_Step 2_

Since in the real experiment the energy of the particles associated to a jet is unknown the \(a_{l}\)'s, obtained in step 1, are expressed as smooth functions of \(E_{kin}^{k}\)

\[a_{l}=g_{l}\Big{(}E_{kin}^{k};b_{l}^{1},...,b_{l}^{n_{l}}\Big{)}\,\qquad(3)\]

where \(n_{l}\) (\(l=1\),..., \(L\)) are the number of parameters appearing in the function. Their value can be obtained fitting equations (3) to the \(a_{l}\)'s values obtained assuming \(E_{kin}^{k}\) known. The following iteration procedure is then used:

1. a starting value for the reconstructed energy is defined. The results are not critical to this value and in the analysis \(E_{start}^{k}=\sum\overline{E}\) was used;
2. the parameters \(a_{l}\)'s are determined using equations (3) and the new reconstructed energy is obtained using equation (1);
3. the weights are re-computed using the energy reconstructed in (b);
4. the procedure is iterated until the change in the reconstructed energy is smaller than 1 _MeV_; typically less than ten iterations are needed.

In references [1, 2], the errors on the parameters \(a_{l}\) were obtained dividing the event sample in 5 sub-samples. Five determinations were then obtained applying the described minimisation to each sub-sample events. The error on the parameter was obtained as the RMS of the five determinations. This procedure is arbitrary and does not allow to take into account parameter correlations in the fits of step 2. In this note a new procedure based on the Maximum Likelihood principle and allowing to take into account correlation is discussed. It was applied to simulated jets [3] and a significant improvement was obtained on the linearity of the reconstructed energy. The jets were produced by quarks having have energies \(E_{0}\) equal to 20, 50, 200 and 1000 \(GeV\) and \(\eta=0.3\). In the application, the _Sampling_ and the _H1_ parameterisations [1, 2] were used in equation (1).

### Estimation of the calibration parameters

The step 1 procedure can be derived using the Maximum Likelihood principle. We assume that the energy measurements are representative of a normal distribution characterised by a mean value \(E_{kin}^{k}\) and a variance \(\sigma\). The Likelihood Function corresponding to the \(n\) measurements is given by

\[L=\prod\limits_{k=1}^{n}\Biggl{[}\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{1\left( E_{rec}^{k}-E_{kin}^{k}\right)^{2}}{\sigma^{2}}}\Biggr{]}. \tag{4}\]

The values of the unknown parameters \(a_{l}\)'s, appearing in the expression of \(E_{rec}^{k}\) correspond to the minimum of the function

\[w=ln\,L=\frac{1}{2\sigma^{2}}\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}\left(E_{rec}^{k}-E_{kin}^{ k}\right)^{2}\,. \tag{5}\]

The minimisation of this equation introduces a systematic error on the reconstructed energy given by [4]

\[\frac{\left\langle E_{kin}^{k}\right\rangle}{\left\langle E_{rec}^{k}\right\rangle }=1+\Biggl{[}\frac{\sigma}{\left\langle E_{rec}^{k}\right\rangle}\Biggr{]}^{2}\]

and then equation (2) is minimised. The estimated values of \(a_{l}\), \(\varepsilon\bigl{(}a_{l}\bigr{)}\), do not depend on \(\sigma\).

### Estimation of the covariance matrix

Using the Maximum Likelihood Principle one can derive now the covariance matrix \(V_{i,j}\) for the parameters \(a_{l}\). For large statistics, \(L=L\bigl{(}a_{1},...,a_{L}\bigr{)}\) is a \(L\)-dimension Gaussian surface and the inverse of the covariance matrix is given by

\[\left(V^{-1}\right)_{i,j}=\left(-\frac{\sigma^{2}w}{\sigma a_{i}\sigma a_{j}} \right)_{\overline{a}=\varepsilon(\overline{a})}\,. \tag{6}\]The matrix is symmetric and the terms are proportional to \(\sigma^{-2}\). This quantity was estimated using the sigma of a gaussian function fitting the distribution \(E_{rec}^{k}-E_{kin}^{k}\) all over the variable domain. In this case, the reconstructed energies were obtained using the estimated values \(\varepsilon\!\left(\overline{a}\right)\) in equation (1). The correlation coefficients are given by

\[\rho_{i,j}=\frac{V_{i,j}}{\sqrt{V_{i,V_{j,j}}}}\;\;. \tag{7}\]

**The _Sampling_ parameterisation**

In the case of the _Sampling_ parameterisation used in reference [2], one has

\[E_{rec}^{k}=a_{1}E_{1}^{k}+a_{2}E_{2}^{k}+a_{3}E_{3}^{k}+a_{4}E_{4}^{k}\]

where \(E_{1}^{k}\), \(E_{2}^{k}\)and \(E_{3}^{k}\) are the energies deposited in the presampler, in the electromagnetic calorimeter and in the hadronic calorimeter respectively and \(E_{4}^{k}=\sqrt{E_{em3}^{k}\times E_{had1}^{k}}\). This term takes into account of the energy lost in the cryostat, \(E_{em3}^{k}\) and \(E_{had1}^{k}\) are the energies deposited in the third sampling of the electromagnetic calorimeter and in the first sampling of the hadronic calorimeter respectively.

In this case, \(L=4\) and the elements of the symmetric matrix \(V^{-1}\) are

\[V^{-1}=\frac{1}{\sigma^{2}}\begin{pmatrix}\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}E_{1}^{k}E_{1} ^{k}\\ \sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}E_{1}^{k}E_{2}^{k}&\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}E_{2}^{k}E_{2}^{k }\\ \sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}E_{1}^{k}E_{3}^{k}&\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}E_{2}^{k}E_{3}^{k }&\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}E_{3}^{k}E_{3}^{k}\\ \sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}E_{1}^{k}E_{4}^{k}&\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}E_{2}^{k}E_{4}^{k }&\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}E_{3}^{k}E_{4}^{k}&\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}E_{4}^{k}E_{4}^ {k}\end{pmatrix}\]

The \(\sigma\) values for the four quark energy values and for \(\Delta R\) equal to 0.4 and 0.7 are shown in table 1 (\(\Delta R\) is the radius of the cone used to reconstruct jets [5]). As an example, we report the matrix elements of the covariance matrix \(V_{i,j}\) obtained for \(E_{0}=200\)\(GeV\) and \(\Delta R=0.7\)

\[V_{200}=\begin{pmatrix}2.16\times 10^{-2}\\ -8.09\times 10^{-4}&6.53\times 10^{-5}\\ -1.96\times 10^{-4}&3.88\times 10^{-6}&1.25\times 10^{-4}\\ -9.42\times 10^{-4}&-2.23\times 10^{-4}&-6.14\times 10^{-4}&6.30\times 10^{-3} \end{pmatrix}.\]The correlation coefficients are given by

\[\rho_{200}=\begin{pmatrix}1&&&&\\ -0.464&1&&\\ -0.014&0.002&1\\ -0.007&-0.121&-0.479&1\end{pmatrix}.\]

#### The \(Hl\) parameterisation

Using the \(Hl\) method [2], one has

\[E_{rec}^{k}=E_{1}^{k}+a_{1}\underset{j}{\sum}E_{cell\,2,j}^{k}+a_{2}\underset{j }{\sum}\frac{E_{cell\,2,j}^{k}}{\left|E_{cell\,2,j}^{k}\right|}+a_{3}\underset {j}{\sum}E_{cell\,3,j}^{k}+a_{4}\underset{j}{\sum}\frac{E_{cell\,3,j}^{k}}{ \left|E_{cell\,3,j}^{k}\right|}+a_{5}E_{4}^{k}\]

where \(E_{cell\,2,j}^{k}\) and \(E_{cell\,3,j}^{k}\) are the energies deposited in the cell \(j\) of the electromagnetic and hadronic calorimeter respectively and, as previously, \(E_{4}^{k}=\sqrt{E_{cm\,3}^{k}\times E_{had\,1}^{k}}\).

In this case, \(L=5\) and the covariance matrix elements are

\[V^{-1}=\frac{1}{\sigma^{2}}\begin{bmatrix}\underset{k=1}{\sum}E_{2}^{k}E_{2} ^{k}&&\\ \underset{k=1}{\sum}E_{2}^{k}H_{2}^{k}&\underset{k=1}{\sum}H_{2}^{k}H_{2}^{k} &&\\ \underset{k=1}{\sum}E_{2}^{k}E_{3}^{k}&\underset{k=1}{\sum}H_{2}^{k}E_{3}^{k} &\underset{k=1}{\sum}E_{3}^{k}E_{3}^{k}&&\\ \underset{k=1}{\sum}E_{2}^{k}H_{3}^{k}&\underset{k=1}{\sum}H_{2}^{k}H_{3}^{k} &\underset{k=1}{\sum}E_{3}^{k}H_{3}^{k}&\underset{k=1}{\sum}H_{3}^{k}H_{3}^{k} \\ \underset{k=1}{\sum}E_{2}^{k}E_{4}^{k}&\underset{k=1}{\sum}H_{2}^{k}E_{4}^{k} &\underset{k=1}{\sum}E_{3}^{k}E_{4}^{k}&\underset{k=1}{\sum}H_{3}^{k}E_{4}^{k }&\underset{k=1}{\sum}E_{4}^{k}E_{4}^{k}\end{bmatrix}\]

where, for \(i\) equal to \(2\) and \(3\), \(E_{i}^{k}=\underset{j}{\sum}E_{cell\,i,j}^{k}\) and \(H_{i}^{k}=\underset{j}{\sum}\frac{E_{cell\,i,j}^{k}}{\left|E_{cell\,i,j}^{k} \right|}\).

**The parameterisation of the \(a_{l}\)'s as function of the energy**

The procedure described in the previous sections allows to determine \(a_{l}\)'s and \(V_{{}_{i,j}}\) for each quark energy. As already discussed, to reconstruct the jet energy without using the values \(E_{kin}^{k}\), it is necessary to express the \(a_{l}\)'s as smooth functions of \(E_{kin}^{k}\) (3).

The values of the parameters \(b_{l}^{n}\)'s appearing in equation (3) are obtained minimising the \(\chi^{2}\) function

\[\chi^{2}=u^{T}\ U^{-1}u\ \,\qquad(8)\]

where \(u\) is the vector

\[u=\begin{pmatrix}&\varepsilon_{20}\bigl{(}a_{1}\bigr{)}-g_{1}\biggl{(}\biggl{(} E_{kin}^{k}\biggr{)}_{20};b_{1}^{1},...,b_{1}^{n_{l}}\biggr{)}\\ &...&\\ &\varepsilon_{20}\bigl{(}a_{L}\bigr{)}-g_{L}\biggl{(}\biggl{(}E_{kin}^{k} \biggr{)}_{20};b_{L}^{1},...,b_{L}^{n_{L}}\biggr{)}\\ &...&\\ &\varepsilon_{1000}\bigl{(}a_{1}\bigr{)}-g_{1}\biggl{(}\biggl{(}E_{kin}^{k} \biggr{)}_{1000};b_{1}^{1},...,b_{1}^{n_{l}}\biggr{)}\\ &...&\\ &\varepsilon_{1000}\bigl{(}a_{L}\bigr{)}-g_{L}\biggl{(}\biggl{(}E_{kin}^{k} \biggr{)}_{1000};b_{L}^{1},...,b_{L}^{n_{l}}\biggr{)}\end{pmatrix},\]\(u^{T}\) is the corresponding raw vector, \(\left\langle E_{kin}^{k}\right\rangle_{i}\) is the average energy of the particles associated to the jets and \(U^{-1}\) is a (\(L\)\(\times\)4) \(\times\) (\(L\)\(\times\)4) symmetric matrix given by

\[U^{-1}=\left(\begin{matrix}V^{-1}\!\left\langle\!\left\langle E_{kin}^{k}\right \rangle_{1}\right\rangle&0&0&0\\ 0&V^{-1}\!\left\langle\!\left\langle E_{kin}^{k}\right\rangle_{2}\right\rangle &0&0\\ 0&0&V^{-1}\!\left\langle\!\left\langle E_{kin}^{k}\right\rangle_{3}\right\rangle &0\\ 0&0&0&V^{-1}\!\left\langle\!\left\langle E_{kin}^{k}\right\rangle_{4}\right\rangle \right\rangle\end{matrix}\right)\]

The 0's indicate \(L\times L\) matrices with all the terms equal to 0.

The results obtained using the "Sampling" and the \(H\!I\) method for \(\Delta R\) equal to 0.4 and 0.7 are here discussed. The functions used in the fits and the obtained parameter values are reported in tables 2 to 5. Table 6 shows the \(\chi^{2}\) probability values. The fitting curves are superimposed to the \(a_{l}\)'s values in figures 1 to 4. The error bars appearing in the figures are the roots of the diagonal elements of the covariance matrices.

## Conclusions

In this note a more rigorous statistical approach for the offline determination of the jet calibration constants is described. It was applied to simulated jets and the results were compared with the ones obtained using a previous procedure [2]. An improvement of more than a factor 2 was obtained in the linearity. The energy resolutions are practically unchanged. Tables 12 and 13 report the weighted averages and the root mean squares of the relative non linearities respectively. In the present analysis more parameters \(\,b_{l}^{n}\,\)'s were used to get good \(\,\chi^{2}\,\)probabilities in the fit. Using the same number of parameters than in reference [2] almost the same improvement in the linearity was found, but the \(\,\chi^{2}\,\)probabilities of the fits were very bad (few %0).
