## 1 Introduction

At the highest luminosities foreseen for LHC the minimum bias events (MB) are produced at an impressive rate : assuming a luminosity

\({\cal L}=1\). \(7~{}10^{34}cm^{-2}sec^{-1}\) and a bunch spacing of 15 ns, a mean number of 20.4 minimum bias events per bunch crossing is expected for an inelastic cross section of 80 mb; assuming instead \({\cal L}=2.5~{}10^{34}cm^{-2}sec^{-1}\), and a bunch spacing of 25 ns,the expected mean number of minimim bias events per bunch crossing is 50. If a bipolar pulse shaping is used for treating the signal, the mean value of the energy for the interesting signals is expected not to be affected, while the \(\sigma(E)\) is expected to be increased by a such a pileup effect [1, 2].

The purpose of this note is to evaluate the pileup contribution in the ATLAS LAr calorimeter (electromagnetic and hadronic parts) as a function of \(\eta\) and of the dimension of the considered region ('cluster region').

## 2 Simulation method

For this study we have used cartridges I28411, I28414, I28492, I28413, I28415, I28493 (file PROD_LOI tapes). The minimum bias events have been generated using PYTHIA 5.5 with G.Ciapetti and A.di Ciaccio's tuning as shown at the Aachen conference [3]. The particles of the generatedevents having a rapidity of \(\pm\) 3 are showered in the fully simulated ATLAS setup.

In this study the longitudinal segmentation of both the electromagnetic and hadronic calorimeters is not used, and all the energies within each calorimeter are added in depth; so the electromagnetic calorimeter and the hadronic one are considered to have just one segment each.

Every generated minimum bias event is assumed to have a weight proportional to the height of the bipolar pulse shape, (Fig.1a and Fig.1b show the pulse shapes for the electromagnetic and hadronic calorimeters taken from the RD3 test beam data). Note that these shapes are the responses to triangular pulses, like the ones describing the calorimeter data; the response to a \(\delta\) function would give a peaking time about half of the peaking time of Figs. 1. Note also, from Fig.1b, that the time shaping of the hadronic pulse is rather long (roughly 1 \(\mu\)sec) : it is the one used for the test beam of '92; the final one for ATLAS has yet been optimized.

The simulation procedure consists of the following steps : the deposited energy of a single minimum bias event in a given (\(\eta\), \(\Delta\phi\times\Delta\eta\)) region is randomly sampled from the distribution obtained from PYTHIA -GEANT for that region (see Fig.2a); to build up this distribution we assume an energy cutoff of 1 MeV instead of 100 KeV (which is the GEANT cutoff), because this choice makes the pileup simulation quite faster and does not affect the resulting RMS, (see Fig.2c and Fig.2d). The energy cutoff is the value of deposited energy below which the cluster region is considered empty and above which is considered hit; given an energy cutoff, every cluster region has its own 'occupation probability', defined as

\[\underline{\underline{number\ of\ hit\ regions}}\]

\[\underline{\underline{number\ of\ regions}}\]

The occupation probability is obviously a function of \(\eta\) and of the size of the region.

## 3 Pileup energy distribution

Weighting the deposited energies of the single MB as described above, the pileup energy distribution is obtained for different cluster regions : as an example, the pileup distribution for the typical electromagnetic cluster ('nonet' ) \(\mid\eta\mid<0.2\), \(\Delta\phi\times\Delta\eta\) =.08\(\times\).08 is shown in Fig.2b.

As far as the electromagnetic calorimeter is concerned, the same simulation method has been used assuming : \({\cal L}\) = 2.5 10\({}^{34}\)\(cm^{-2}\)\(sec^{-1}\), 25 nsec as bunch spacing and the pulse shaping of Fig.1a sampled every 25 nsec, i.e. the pulse shaping of Fig.1c. The resulting pileup distribution for the 'nonet' is shown in Fig.2e.

Note that the mean increase in RMS is a factor 1.15, whithout strong dependence of the cluster region size (Fig.6), to be compared with the factor 1.21 (ratio of the square root of the luminosities) that would be expected on the basis of the change in luminosity only; so the effect of the pure change in bunch frequency is negligible.

For the jet reconstruction the typical size parameter used is

\(\Delta R=\sqrt{\Delta\phi^{2}+\Delta\eta^{2}}\), where R is the radius of the angular cone (half aperture angle of the cone) within which the jet energy is collected; so for the regions to be used for jet reconstruction ('jet clusters') the area is A = \(\Delta R^{2}\)\(\pi\). We considered square cluster regions, having linear dimension x = \(\Delta R\sqrt{\pi}\), i.e. the same area A = \(\Delta R^{2}\)\(\pi\).

The energy deposition of the single minimum bias and of the pileup for the cluster region \(\mid\eta\mid<0.2\), \(\Delta\phi\times\Delta\eta\) =. 88\(\times\).88 (i.e. \(\Delta R=0.5\)) in the hadronic calorimeter, are shown in Fig.3a and Fig.3b.

To give an idea of the dependence of the pileup distribution on the shaping time, a test has been done assuming the electromagnetic one (unre-alytically fast) for the hadronic cluster of Figs.3. The resulting pileup distribution is in Fig.4 : the RMS is decreased by a factor 2.

## 4 Results

Note that from now on all the results about RMS and energy are'reconstructed' energies. The coefficients of such a reconstruction are assumed to be the same found for 50 GeV jets generated in the same setup (file I28402), according to the expression :

\[{\bf E}_{\rm rec}={\bf c}_{\rm p}^{\rm j}{\bf E}_{\rm p}+{\bf c}_{\rm e}^{\rm j }{\bf E}_{\rm e}+{\bf c}_{\rm h}^{\rm j}{\bf E}_{\rm h}\]

where \({\bf c}_{\rm p}^{j}=14.5,\;{\bf c}_{\rm e}^{j}=7.7,\;{\bf c}_{\rm h}^{j}=28.9\),

\(E_{\rm rec}=\) reconstructed energy,

\(E_{\rm p},\;E_{\rm e},\;E_{\rm h}=\) deposited energy in preshower, electromagnetic

and hadronic calorimeter, respectively.

Note also that the three largest regions \(\Delta\phi\times\Delta\eta=.5\times.5,.88\times.88,1.24\times 1.24\) correspond to \(\Delta R=.3\),.5,.7 respectively.

A possibility would be to use the RMS of a truncated distribution : calling \(\sigma\) the standard deviation and \(x\) the mean value of the gaussian fit of the pileup energy distribution, the RMS obtained considering such a distribution only from \(x-3\sigma\) to \(x+3\sigma\) has been estimated. With this procedure, the fraction of considered events is roughly 92% and the estimated RMS values are only slightly bigger (\(\sim 10\%\)) than the ones obtained by the gaussian fits (see Tab.3).

Another important point to be considered is the fact that the multiplicity of the minimum bias we use is clearly understimated : Fig.10 shows the rapidity distribution we use compared to the one given by DTUJET [5] (the generator used in FLUKA). The value at \(\eta=0\) given by Pythia is around 4.0 particles for unit of rapidity, while the other one is \(\sim 6.2\). A value greater than 6 is also to be expected on the basis of CDF experimental data [6], where the value 3.95 is found at \(\sqrt{s}=1.8\) TeV and a value 6.4 is expected at \(\sqrt{s}=18\) TeV, extrapolating at \(\sqrt{s}\ >1.8\) TeV the function that fits the data at \(\sqrt{s}\leq 1.8\) GeV. An increase of a factor \(\sim 1.5\) in the multiplicity would give an increase of a factor \(\sim 20\%\) on the \(\sigma(E)\) due to pileup.

## 5 Conclusion

As a conclusion we give our best estimation of the \(\sigma(E)\) due to pileup for electrons (using the reconstruction factor 6.5 suited for electrons, instead of 7.7 found for jets) and for jets, at \(\eta\simeq 0\) and \(\eta\simeq 2.5\); for both electrons and jet the full RMS is used. The correction factor 1.2 is applied for minimum bias multiplicity.

\[\eta\ \simeq\ 0\]

Electron region is the 'nonet' \(\Delta\phi\times\Delta\eta=.08\times.08\) ( \(\sim 10.5\times 10.5\ cm\))

\[\sigma(E)=163.3\times 1.2\times\frac{6.5}{7.7}\simeq 160\ MeV\]

\(\sigma(E)\) would decrease by a factor \(\sim 2\) if the 'truncated' RMS would be used.

'Jet regions' :

\[\Delta R =.3 \sigma(E)=2.2\oplus 2.5 =3.3\ \mbox{GeV}\] \[\Delta R =.5 \sigma(E)=4.2\oplus 4.4 =6.1\ \mbox{GeV}\] \[\Delta R =.7 \sigma(E)=8.3\oplus 7.9 =11.5\ \mbox{GeV}\]

The quadratic sum is valid in the case of no correlation between the pileup energy depositions in the electromagnetic calorimeter and the hadronic one. For the energy depositions of a single MB a negative correlation exists, and in presence of negative correlations the quadratic sum gives an upper limit for \(\sigma(E)\). However the use of different shaping times for the hadronic and electromagnetic calorimeters should minimize the correlation, randomizing the ratio between electromagnetic and hadronic time weights.

\[\eta\ \simeq\ 2.5\]

The electron region cannot be chosen as \(\Delta\phi\times\Delta\eta=.08\times.08\) because the size in centimeters is too small to contain the electron ( \(\sim 4.9\times 4.6\ cm\)). We choose a region where each angular dimension is multiplied by 1.5 giving a size in centimeters similar to the 'nonet' of the test beam '92 (\(7.2\times 8\ cm\)), where the 92% of the electron energy was contained.

\[\sigma(E)=911.7\times 1.2\times\frac{6.5}{7.7}\times 1.5\simeq 1.4\ GeV\]

\[\sigma(E_{i})=229\ MeV\]

'Jet regions' :

\(\Delta R=.3\)\(\sigma(E)=11.4\oplus 22=24.8\) GeV

\(\sigma(E_{i})=4\) GeV

\(\Delta R=.5\)\(\sigma(E)=19.7\oplus 40=44.6\) GeV

\(\sigma(E_{i})=7.3\) GeV

\(\Delta R=.7\)\(\sigma(E)=39.2\oplus 65=76\) GeV

\(\sigma(E_{i})=12.4\) GeV
