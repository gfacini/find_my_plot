**Pre-study of an**

**All Liquid Argon Calorimeter**

**with**

**Integrated Solenoid**

This note is a report of the studies on the feasability of an all-liquid argon calorimeter based on three elements which, taken together, provide an elegant solution for an experiment at LHC (although several aspects of the study could also be used separately):

- Accordion structure for the hadronic compartments a la RD3

- Minimization of the amount of dead material and dead-space between barrel and end-caps by thin flat walls for the cryostats.

- Integration of a small-radius solenoid in the cryostat vacuum.

Its goal is to set the basis of a reasonable design, which can be used in a first step in simulations and in discussions about the integration with the rest of the experiment and with external constraints. It is not intended to give final design numbers, and points like thicknesses, dead spaces etc... can probably be further optimized if found critical.

The introduction gives a short report (in English), then there are 6 notes in French.

- about the following mechanical aspects (engineer J. M. Baze ):

1. General considerations on the structure

2. Behaviour of the elementary module of 5 hadronic sectors a la RD3

3. Cable routing and design of the end plates of the modules.

4. Taking care of the displacements of the flat end-walls of the vacuum vessel

and of the vacuum separation for the solenoid.

5. First study of the buckling of the cold central tube.

.6 design parameters for the cryogenics. (engineers JP Lottin and A Patoux). one minimum of 2.4 bars, which assumes that there is a liquid / gas surface of Argon just above the modules, and a gas pressure of 1.4 bars, necessary for operating the safety valve.

. one maximum of 3.86 bars (precise number for historical reasons) which provides a very comfortable overpressure (for example for preventing the apparition of bubbles, see later)

- the length over which the tube holds by itself : 8 meters for a tube just held at its two ends, 4 meters if a support can be found in the middle etc..

- of course the detailed shape of the wall

- the initial ovalisation, taken to be 1 %.

- the safety factor, between breakdown pressure and operating pressure, which is 3.

The finite elements analysis (more precise) gives slightly lower values ( example 15 mm fo L = 8000 mm).

An interesting solution which holds is a tube thinner towards the ends ( 15 mm between z = 0. and z = 2500 mm, and 9 mm between z = 2500 and z = 4000.

Some solutions with corrugations or stiffening circles have been studied (not all reported in the note. The main messages are :

For a standalone tube, one needs a large radial space ( about 100 mm) if one wants to significantly decrease the average thickness. Less radial space leads to an average thickness close to the one of a smooth tube, but with an inhomogeneous distribution.

A better solution can probably be reached if the tube is linked with the e-m calorimeter, on the same principle that for the end-walls. This point should be studied further.

The superconducting coil, providing a field of 2 T, is a 5 cm thick Al tube. Radial space before and after it are 70 mm. The coil is hanged at the ends by tie rods. Horizontal rods hold it in place along the z-axis. It is proposed to have a separate vacuum for the coil, in order to reach a lower pressure than in the insulating vacuum of the cryostat. The coil connections (He, current, vacuum) run along the end-walls of the calorimeter inside the vacuum. It is proposed to have only a thermal screen between the coil and the warm tube, not between the coil and the argon vessel, thus taking full advantage of the integration of the coil in the cryostat. The consequence is that the coil cannot be operated if the cryostat is not cold, which should not be a real problem (the coil has been tested in a separate vacuum enclosure before installation).
